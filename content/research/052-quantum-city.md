## Abstract

Version 2 — substantially rewritten from the inside out. Urban infrastructure commissioned in 2026 will operate into the 2070s, yet the cryptography protecting it and the computation orchestrating it refresh on decade-scale cycles. This whitepaper follows one Tuesday through three coexisting cities — the Excel City of the past, the edge-native city being built now, and the front-running city of 2046 — to show what quantum computing, self-healing networks (TM Forum Autonomous Networks L0-L5), and interoperable digital twins change for a nurse, a plant manager, a life-sciences researcher, a network operator, and a citizen. It grounds the argument in the SMILE v4.0 methodology (Waern, 2026): six phases ending in Perpetual Wisdom, AEST temporal operations (Absorb, Emulate, Simulate, Transcend), SPIN twinning dimensions (Space, Place, Interaction, Network), the NUDEDA need-first maturity lens, and reality as the boundary-spanning object between communities that share no vocabulary. It documents why current practice — spreadsheet-run operations, planning data with holes, statistics instead of reality-grounded simulation, architectures where data leaves to be harvested and later decrypted — cannot carry the coming decades; quantifies the operator economics of ARPU, churn, and self-healing autonomy; traces 6G densification toward holographic-type communication as the physical layer of a holographic society; and treats Minimal Interoperability Mechanisms as constitutional infrastructure. ## 1. Introduction: A Tale of Three Cities, Told from Inside

### 1.1 The Same Tuesday, Three Times

Consider Miriam, a charge nurse, and her Tuesday. She works it three times — once in the city of the past, once in the city being built now, once in the city of 2046. The hospital is the same building. Her patients are the same patients. What differs is whether reality is allowed to participate in the decisions made about it.

In the first city, Miriam's Tuesday runs on artifacts about reality rather than reality itself. Bed status lives in a spreadsheet updated when someone remembers — and, as Chapter 2 audits in detail, roughly 86–94% of operational spreadsheets contain at least one error, with developers systematically overconfident about their own work [demonstrated] (Panko, 2016). Her sepsis screening is a paper checklist run at shift change, hours after the physiology has already moved. When the regional carrier's network drops — the kind of single-misconfiguration outage Chapter 2 costs out — her telemetry goes silent and nobody upstream notices until she calls. The deeper problem is epistemic, not technical: this city excludes the world from the very observations it makes about the world. Its instruments record opinions about last Tuesday, and the macro-scale price of that habit is quantified in Chapter 2.

In the second city — the one being built now, unevenly — Miriam's Tuesday is partially inhabited by a living model. Hospitals that operationalized this earliest show what the difference is worth. The Johns Hopkins Capacity Command Center, a digital twin of hospital operations live since February 2016, assigns emergency-admitted patients a bed 30% faster, cut operating-room-to-floor transfer delays 70%, and dispatches critical-care transport 63 minutes sooner, while the hospital runs above 95% occupancy [deployed] (Johns Hopkins Medicine, 2021; GE HealthCare Command Center, n.d.); Cleveland Clinic's Virtual Command Center independently validates the pattern [deployed] (Cleveland Clinic, 2024). Miriam's sepsis alarm is no longer a checklist: across 590,736 monitored patients, the TREWS system flagged sepsis a median 5.7 hours earlier and, where clinicians confirmed the alert within three hours, delivered an 18.7% relative reduction in in-hospital mortality [deployed] (Adams et al., 2022); a second deep-learning deployment at UC San Diego cut sepsis mortality roughly 17% relative [deployed] (Boussina et al., 2024), and Duke's Sepsis Watch showed such systems can enter nursing workflow without increasing workload [deployed] (Sendak et al., 2020). The network beneath her, in the best current cases, heals itself: TM Forum-validated Level 4 autonomy for a single use case — RAN energy efficiency (scenario GB1059H) — in a live commercial Open RAN delivers roughly 20% RAN energy savings through AI closed-loop control on production traffic [deployed] (Rakuten Mobile, 2026) — though independent analysis cautions most operators remain at L2–L3 with savings lagging vendor projections [demonstrated] (Bain & Company, 2024). Gibson's future has arrived in Miriam's second city the way it always arrives: unevenly, one ward, one cell site, one procurement at a time.

In the third city — 2046, and this paper labels every claim about it [projection] — Miriam does not consult representations at all. She queries reality. The ward's twin runs continuously ahead of physical events in Grieves's replicate–predict–navigate sense (Grieves, 2023), and the question "which patient deteriorates tonight?" is answered by a simulation that has never lost correlation with the physics-based ground truth. Her own hallway conversations with a remote specialist ride holographic-type communication whose requirements — multi-sense stream synchronization, sub-20 ms latency — today exist only as pre-standards work [projection] (ITU-T Focus Group on Technologies for Network 2030, 2020), against per-hologram bandwidth estimates that still exceed standardized 6G capability targets by orders of magnitude [projection] (Taleb et al., 2022; ITU-R, 2023).

### 1.2 Reality as the Primary Construct

The thread connecting Miriam's three Tuesdays is a single architectural commitment this paper advances: reality is the primary construct. This framing is our own — we build it on Waern's (2026) SMILE formulation that reality digitally rendered is "the ultimate boundary object" and the nexus through which knowledge transfer happens, and extend it to a working design principle: reality persists irrespective of our instruments, and every dashboard, spreadsheet, model, and forecast is a secondary, corrigible instrument that must be calibrated against it — never the reverse. The first city inverts this ordering and pays for it. The SMILE lens is the author's proposed methodology, which the author operates in production smart-building deployments — edge-native local actuation with incremental learning, insights leaving the premises only under differential privacy — rather than as settled theory; the reader should treat its constructs as this paper's contribution to be evaluated, not as established method.

Five SMILE constructs carry the argument. First, NUDEDA: proceed from an identified NEED toward data requirements, never data-first — the corrective to the documented failure mode in which planning tools underperform precisely because data collection preceded any stated question [demonstrated] (NetSuite, 2024). Second, SPIN twinning: every twin addresses Space (the objective, measurable physical environment), Place (its humanly meaningful interpretation — Miriam's ward is also a jurisdiction and a community of care), Interaction (the exchange among people, systems, and AI), and Network (the ecosystem of interconnected twins). Third, AEST, available at any anchor point: Absorb the past, Emulate the living present, Simulate forward futures, and Transcend — reverse-engineer the path from the desired sustainable future back to present action. Transcendence is structured reverse causation from a defined target state, operating across both real and fictional representations so long as correlation to physical possibility is maintained. Fourth, Actor-Network Theory grounds why this works socially: everything — nurse, sensor, algorithm, hospital board — is an actor in a sociotechnological ecosystem, and to think outside the box one must define the boundary box first. Reality, digitally rendered, is the boundary-spanning object all communities share regardless of vocabulary; the governance collapse of Sidewalk Toronto illustrates what happens when data governance is designed before any such shared object exists [demonstrated] (Goodman & Powles, 2019). Fifth, the Crucible trust layer — retrieval over generation, explainability by design — governs every AI output, in the Web4/Physical AI paradigm where physical and digital exchange continuously and intelligence lives at the edge because that is where reality happens; centralized alternatives concentrate failure, as Chapter 2 quantifies.

### 1.3 Why These Decisions Persist for Decades

SMILE's six phases end in Phase 6, Perpetual Wisdom, whose exit condition Waern (2026) states as knowledge that persists beyond project closure — cross-industry templates and reusable strategies that outlive their origin. This paper extends Phase 6's knowledge-persistence commitment to what we call perpetual reality recording: once a city records reality continuously, every architecture decision becomes permanent institutional memory. The ontology chosen in 2026 is the ontology every 2046 query traverses; the interoperability boundary never architected is the boundary at which Miriam's cross-domain question fails. Permanence also has a shadow — data recorded today under quantum-vulnerable encryption is already being harvested for later decryption, making durable recordings long-horizon security liabilities from the moment of capture [deployed] (National Security Agency, 2021; Ott et al., 2025). Buildings and grids live half-centuries; the software governing them refreshes in single-digit years; the recordings outlive both. Ontology and interoperability choices therefore persist across 50-year asset lifecycles, and the chapters that follow examine what deciding well requires.

## 2. Why Today Won't Cut It: The Excel City

Before any city earns the adjective "quantum," an audit of the tools that actually run today's urban infrastructure is owed — and the audit is damning. Strip away the dashboards and the procurement language, and the operational substrate of most municipal energy planning, maintenance budgeting, and capacity forecasting is the spreadsheet. The evidence on that substrate is unambiguous [demonstrated]: cell-by-cell audits and field studies converge on the finding that roughly 86–94% of operational spreadsheets contain at least one error, that in any large model at least one incorrect bottom-line value is very likely, that errors resist detection, and that developers are systematically overconfident about all of the above (Panko, 2016). The city of 2026 is, operationally, an Excel city: an infrastructure with fifty-year asset lives steered by planning artifacts with a near-certain embedded defect rate that nobody has budgeted to find.

The defect rate has a price. Poor data quality costs the U.S. economy an oft-cited estimated $3.1 trillion annually through lost productivity, outages, and knowledge-worker time spent correcting bad data rather than using it (an IBM estimate reported in Redman, 2016) [estimate]; at firm level, Gartner's survey of 154 reference customers puts the average cost at no less than $12.9 million per organization per year [demonstrated] (Gartner, 2021). These are not the costs of missing data. They are the costs of *data-first* practice: collect everything, govern later, and let quality be discovered downstream, at decision time, by the decision failing. SMILE's NUDEDA lens names the corrective — proceed from an identified NEED toward data requirements, never data-first (Waern, 2026). The inverted pyramid (Outcome → Action → Insight → Information → Data) is the only ordering under which a data-quality defect is discoverable *before* it becomes an operational one, because every data element carries a traceable justification back to the outcome it serves. Practitioner evidence confirms the failure mode in the wild: incomplete order histories and departmentally siloed, poorly governed data produce skewed demand signals and inaccurate planning even where advanced software is deployed [demonstrated] (NetSuite, 2024) — the tooling improved, the epistemology did not.

### Regressing the dead past

The Excel city's second failure is methodological, and it survives the migration to machine learning intact. The dominant analytical mode of urban and network planning is statistical: fit a curve to yesterday, extrapolate to tomorrow. The theory is clear on when this breaks. Out-of-sample forecast performance deteriorates sharply under parameter nonconstancy and structural breaks — precisely the conditions a city under renovation, densification, climate shift, and behavioral change permanently exhibits [peer-reviewed-theory] (Xu & Perron, 2019). Worse, in complex systems with emergent, non-linear, long-range-dependent behavior, distinct stochastic models can become statistically *indistinguishable* from finite data — an epistemic ceiling on curve-fitting that no additional data volume lifts [preprint] (arXiv:2509.24977, 2025). A regression trained on a building's pre-retrofit heating seasons is not a cautious model of its post-retrofit behavior; it is a confident model of a building that no longer exists.

The SMILE correction is the AEST temporal operation set: Absorb the past, but *Emulate the living present* — a present-state twin continuously calibrated against physics-based ground truth — then Simulate forward futures and Transcend by reverse-engineering the path from the desired sustainable future back to present action (Waern, 2026). This is the difference between statistics and simulation: the regression asks what yesterday implies; the emulated twin asks what the system, as it actually is right now, will do next — Grieves's (2023) replicate–predict–navigate cycle run ahead of physical reality rather than behind it. Reality is the primary construct; every model fitted to its historical exhaust is a secondary, corrigible instrument, and must be architected as replaceable the moment reality contradicts it.

### Architectures that ship the crown jewels

The third failure is topological. The default architecture of the Excel city sends data *away* — to a vendor cloud, to a centralized region, to an analytics platform — for insight to be generated far from the point of action. The concentration risk is now documented at scale. The July 2024 CrowdStrike faulty update crashed roughly 8.5 million Windows devices and cost Fortune 500 firms an estimated $5.4 billion in direct losses, grounding flights and downing 911 centers from a single distribution point [demonstrated] (Parametrix, 2024). A single AWS region failure cascaded simultaneously through Roblox, Snapchat, Slack, and dozens of dependent services [demonstrated] (Digital Watch Observatory, 2025). Unplanned IT downtime averages $14,056 per minute — $23,750 for large enterprises [demonstrated] (BigPanda, 2024); and the FCC's report on the February 2024 AT&T nationwide outage — a roughly 12-hour event triggered by one misconfiguration — found all voice and 5G data service down, more than 125 million devices affected, over 92 million calls blocked, and 25,000-plus failed 911 attempts [demonstrated] (Federal Communications Commission, 2024). Fifty-four percent of organizations report their most recent significant outage cost over $100,000, and telecoms and third-party digital service providers account for roughly two-thirds of publicly reported outages tracked over nine years [deployed] (Uptime Institute, 2025). And when a city decides to leave, the exit is tolled: egress fees of roughly $0.087–$0.12/GB mean repatriating 50 TB costs $3,500–$7,000 before a single engineering hour — lock-in as a line item, now under EU antitrust scrutiny [demonstrated] (CloudOptimo, 2024).

Centralization also converts today's data into tomorrow's breach. NSA, CISA, and NIST warn that adversaries already harvest encrypted traffic to decrypt once cryptographically relevant quantum computers arrive [deployed] (National Security Agency, 2021). CNSA 2.0, released in 2022, accordingly mandates a phased transition to post-quantum algorithms — software and firmware signing from 2025, support in new equipment across categories through roughly 2025–2027, and exclusive CNSA 2.0 use across national security systems required by 2033 [deployed] (National Security Agency, 2022). Peer-reviewed analysis frames the arithmetic starkly: with 10–15-year quantum timelines and 5–10-year cryptographic migration cycles, long-lived data intercepted today is *already* exposed [peer-reviewed-theory] (Ott et al., 2025). This lands with particular force on Phase 6's commitment to knowledge that persists beyond project closure (Waern, 2026), which this paper extends to perpetual reality recording. A perpetual recording of a city's occupancy, utility, and health-adjacent telemetry is exactly the long-horizon asset harvest-now-decrypt-later targets. The architectural conclusion is not to record less; it is to record *locally*. Raw data stays at the edge where it was born; only formulated problems and privacy-protected aggregates cross the boundary. What never leaves cannot be harvested in transit.

### Governance designed for paper

The final failure is institutional, and Toronto is its canonical exhibit. The Sidewalk Labs Quayside project was cancelled in 2020 amid COVID-era economic uncertainty, after two years of escalating governance and privacy controversy in which its proposed Urban Data Trust could not articulate what problem it solved, to whom it was accountable, or how it related to existing data-protection law, and had already cost it its Privacy-by-Design advisers including Ann Cavoukian [demonstrated] (Goodman & Powles, 2019). The governance instrument was, in effect, a paper-era artifact bolted onto a sensing-era proposal — and the actor-network rejected the transplant. Read through SMILE's Actor-Network Theory foundation, the diagnosis is precise: everything is actors in sociotechnological ecosystems, and a data-governance body that has not been enrolled as an obligatory passage point by the communities it claims to govern collects legitimacy the way an unenrolled sensor network collects data — in response to questions nobody posed (Waern, 2026). The corrective is to define the boundary box before thinking outside it: establish reality itself as the boundary-spanning object that residents, regulators, and operators read differently but share physically, and gate every AI output through a Crucible-style trust layer — retrieval over generation, provenance on every data point — rather than asking citizens to trust an institutional abstraction.

Each failure above has the same shape: a secondary instrument — the spreadsheet, the regression, the central cloud, the paper trust — was mistaken for the primary construct. The chapters that follow build in the opposite order, from reality outward, and reference these figures rather than re-quoting them.

## 3. The Personal Layer: A Day in Two Realities

Architecture debates become abstract unless they land on a person. This chapter runs the same day twice — for the same fictional but data-anchored resident, "Elin," a 58-year-old nurse in a mid-sized Nordic city — once in 2026 as systems actually behave today, and once in 2036 under the architecture this paper argues for. The 2036 half is a Transcend exercise in the SMILE sense: borrowing from the sustainable future to build it now, reverse-engineering the path from the desired future state back to present action (Waern, 2026). Every 2026 figure is labeled [deployed] or [demonstrated]; every 2036 figure is a labeled [projection] derived from those anchors, not invented. The split-screen also operationalizes SMILE's real–fictional dynamic: a simulated future that has never existed can still be emulated against physics-based ground truth, so long as it maintains correlation to physical possibility (Waern, 2026).

### 3.1 Health: The Appointment Versus the Negotiation

**2026.** Elin's mother deteriorates on a Tuesday. The care pathway is reactive: symptoms are noticed by humans, escalated by phone, and adjudicated in an emergency department whose bed-assignment latency is itself a clinical variable. The state of the art is already better than the median — Johns Hopkins' Capacity Command Center assigns ED-admitted patients a bed 30% faster and dispatches critical-care transport 63 minutes sooner while running above 95% occupancy [deployed] (Johns Hopkins Medicine, 2021; GE HealthCare Command Center, n.d.), and machine-learning early warning has crossed from pilot to outcome: TREWS flagged sepsis a median 5.7 hours earlier across 590,736 monitored patients, with an 18.7% relative mortality reduction among confirmed alerts [deployed] (Adams et al., 2022); COMPOSER cut ED sepsis mortality ~17% relative at UC San Diego [deployed] (Boussina et al., 2024); Duke's Sepsis Watch showed such systems can enter nurse workflow without added workload [deployed] (Sendak et al., 2020). But these are institutional twins. Elin's mother benefits only once she is *inside* the instrumented building. Her own body carries no continuously learning representation; her data sits in silos whose quality profile is the industry norm Chapter 2 costed out.

**2036.** Elin's mother has a personal twin that has run for a decade under what this paper calls perpetual reality recording — knowledge persisting beyond any single care episode, because in this architecture reality is the primary construct and every application consuming it is a secondary, corrigible instrument. When her overnight physiology drifts, the personal twin — running AEST's Simulate operation locally — front-runs the deterioration in the Grieves (2023) replicate–predict–navigate sense and opens a negotiation with the hospital ward's operational twin. Critically, *no raw data leaves the home node*. The personal twin computes locally and releases only differentially private insight summaries — the "data stays home; insights travel" contract — so the ward twin learns "incoming patient, sepsis-risk trajectory class 3, ETA 40 minutes" without ever holding the underlying time series. The ward twin, descendant of the Hopkins/Cleveland Clinic command-center lineage [deployed] (Cleveland Clinic, 2024), pre-assigns the bed the way Hopkins already does 30% faster today. The clinical substance is plausible because its components are individually real: patient-specific cardiac digital twins already guided ablation in an FDA-permitted single-arm pilot (n=10, no control), with eight of ten patients recurrence-free at one year [demonstrated] (Trayanova et al., 2025), and regulator-qualified digital-twin methods already substitute for up to 33% of control-arm patients in Phase 3 trials [demonstrated] (Fisher et al., 2024; Venkatesh et al., 2024). What 2036 adds is not new physics — it is MIM-style minimal interoperability between a sovereign personal twin and an institutional twin that were never designed by the same vendor.

### 3.2 Home: The Thermostat Versus the Building That Remembers

**2026.** Elin's apartment building runs a static schedule tuned at commissioning and never revisited — the operational equivalent of the error-riddled spreadsheet Chapter 2 audits. The building forgets its own history annually.

**2036.** The building has executed AEST's Absorb operation on ten years of its own recorded reality: every heating curve, every occupancy rhythm, every façade-solar interaction is institutional memory encoded in the artifact, not in a retired technician's head. It Emulates its living present, Simulates the coming cold front against district-grid price signals, and pre-heats thermal mass six hours early — front-running comfort rather than reacting to complaint. The pattern is a domestic-scale analogue of results already on the books in industry: Siemens' twin-fed predictive maintenance cut unexpected turbine breakdowns 20% and reports up to 30% operational-cost reduction in some facilities [projection for the residential case; the underlying figures are deployed industrial results, not a residential trial] (Siemens AG, 2024). The magnitude of any household energy saving remains unquantified pending an actual residential smart-heating trial.

### 3.3 Mobility: Autonomy as Measured Outcome

**2026.** Elin's commute fails visibly several times a year. The failure economics are the ones Chapter 2 documents: unplanned IT downtime averaging $14,056 per minute, and national-scale carrier outages triggered by single misconfigurations. Network quality is consistently among the top two or three drivers of smartphone-user churn [industry-estimate] (Vecta Labs, n.d.) — meaning Elin, statistically, may already have switched carriers over exactly this.

**2036.** During her tram ride, a radio unit serving the corridor begins to fail. The network — operating toward the autonomy levels TM Forum-validated deployments already foreshadow, where Rakuten Mobile's L4 closed loop for RAN energy efficiency runs on production traffic with no measured customer-experience impact [deployed] (Rakuten Mobile, 2026) and China Mobile reports an 80% reduction in major faults [demonstrated] (Fierce Network, 2025) — had already optimized mobility-robustness parameters in its digital twin. The failing cell drains; neighbors absorb; the swap is dispatched. Autonomy here is not a perfection claim but a measured L4 outcome: roughly 20% energy reduction and materially fewer mobility-caused failures. Elin's call does not drop, and she never learns the event occurred. The absence of failure is the product — which is precisely why it is hard to sell and easy to under-invest in, a pricing problem Chapter 6 takes up.

### 3.4 The Ledger, and a Life in SPIN

Summing labeled magnitudes: hours (command-center-class flow gains of 21–30% on admission and discharge latency [deployed] translate, for one serious family care episode, to a day of waiting recovered [projection]); health-years (a 17–19% relative mortality reduction on time-critical conditions is already demonstrated at hospital scale [deployed]; extending detection upstream to the home is the [target]); money (energy and retention benefits that Chapters 3.2 and 6 develop, kept out of this ledger as magnitudes because their supporting figures are extrapolations or industry estimates rather than measured residential or P&L results).

SMILE's SPIN dimensions, usually applied to districts, apply to a person. **Space:** Elin's body and apartment as objectively measurable physical environments — the domain of sensors and physics-based models, indifferent to interpretation. **Place:** the same square meters as humanly meaningful — "home," "mother's bedside" — the interpretations a ward twin must respect when it negotiates rather than commands. **Interaction:** the four channels (People, Systems, Planet, AI) through which her decisions flow into her twins and recommendations flow back, always overridable. **Network:** her personal twin as one node among the building's, the ward's, and the city's — the ecosystem within which, per the NUDEDA lens, every data flow traces back to an identified need (her mother's survival, her own warmth) rather than to data-first accumulation. Elin never sees the architecture. That is the point: architecture decisions made in 2026 determine whether her 2036 Tuesday is a negotiation between sovereign twins or another phone call to a full emergency department.

## 4. The Hospital: From Ward Rounds to Front-Running Care

### 4.1 The hospital of the past: data leaves, wisdom evaporates

The twentieth-century hospital ran on ward rounds and retrospection. Clinicians recorded observations at the bedside, clerks transcribed them, and the data promptly left the building — to payers, registries, and quarterly quality reports that described a hospital that no longer existed by the time anyone read them. Operational planning lived in spreadsheets, whose audited error profile Chapter 2 shows to be disqualifying, at a macro bill the same chapter costs out. Worse than the errors was the amnesia. When a charge nurse who could feel a bed crisis coming eight hours out retired, the hospital's most accurate forecasting model walked out the door. Nothing recorded reality; everything recorded documents about reality, and the documents were the product. In SMILE terms, the past hospital inverted NUDEDA: it proceeded from available data toward whatever utility could be salvaged, rather than from identified need toward data requirements (Waern, 2026) — the same failure mode operational-forecasting literature documents in manufacturing, where siloed, incomplete histories produce skewed signals regardless of software sophistication [demonstrated] (NetSuite, 2024).

### 4.2 The hospital of 2026: command centers and measured mortality

The present tense is more instructive than the sales decks admit, because the deployed results are strong enough to need no embellishment. Johns Hopkins' Judy Reitz Capacity Command Center — a GE HealthCare digital twin of hospital operations live since February 2016 — assigns ED-admitted patients a bed 30% faster, transfers patients 26% faster after bed assignment, cut OR-to-floor transfer delays 70%, dispatches critical-care transport 63 minutes sooner, and discharges 21% more patients before noon, all while running above 95% occupancy [deployed] (Johns Hopkins Medicine, 2021; GE HealthCare Command Center, n.d.). Cleveland Clinic's Virtual Command Center independently replicates the pattern, fusing EHR, bed-tracking, and transfer feeds into real-time census and enterprise capacity forecasting [deployed] (Cleveland Clinic, 2024). This is Grieves's replicate–predict–navigate cycle (Grieves, 2023) running against beds instead of networks: the twin replicates occupancy, predicts the bottleneck, and navigates the transfer before the corridor fills.

Clinical prediction has crossed the same threshold, with mortality numbers rather than throughput numbers. The TREWS sepsis early-warning system, monitoring 590,736 patients across five hospitals, flagged sepsis a median 5.7 hours earlier and — for the 6,877 septic patients whose alert a clinician confirmed within three hours — delivered a 3.3% absolute (18.7% relative) reduction in in-hospital mortality at 89% provider adoption [deployed] (Adams et al., 2022). UC San Diego's COMPOSER deployment cut sepsis mortality from a counterfactual 11.39% to an observed 9.49% across 6,217 septic patients [deployed] (Boussina et al., 2024), and Duke's Sepsis Watch demonstrated that such systems can integrate into nursing workflow without measured workload increase [deployed] (Sendak et al., 2020). Note what the adoption figures encode: the decisive variable in all three studies was whether a human confirmed the alert. The four SMILE perspectives — From People, From Systems, From Planet, From AI — are not a courtesy here; the mortality reduction literally materializes only through the People channel (Waern, 2026).

One tier further in, the patient herself becomes the twinned anchor. In an FDA-permitted ten-patient Johns Hopkins pilot (single-arm, uncontrolled), patient-specific electromechanical heart twins built from cardiac MRI were used to virtually induce ventricular tachycardia and derive ablation targets; eight of ten patients had no recurrence at one year [demonstrated] (Trayanova et al., 2025). In trials, EMA-qualified prognostic digital twins reduced required control-arm enrollment by up to 33% in a Johnson & Johnson Phase 3 Alzheimer's collaboration [demonstrated] (Fisher et al., 2024; Venkatesh et al., 2024).

### 4.3 Data closer to the heart: sovereignty as clinical architecture

The architectural decision that separates the 2026 pilots from the 2040 hospital is where the recording lives. Health records are the longest-lived sensitive data any city holds, which makes centralized aggregation a compounding liability twice over: operationally — the concentration failures Chapter 2 documents (CrowdStrike, AWS-region cascades) halt surgeries as readily as they ground flights — and cryptographically, because harvest-now-decrypt-later means a patient record intercepted in transit today is already exposed against 10–15-year quantum timelines [peer-reviewed-theory] (Ott et al., 2025; National Security Agency, 2021). Egress pricing adds a third, quieter mechanism: it makes repatriating clinical data economically punitive by design [demonstrated] (CloudOptimo, 2024). The sovereign alternative follows the stack developed in Chapter 8: the perpetual recording of ward reality stays on hospital premises; what crosses the boundary are differentially private aggregates, federated model updates, and formulated problem instances. Hospitals interoperate the way MIM-compliant cities do — through minimal mechanisms and shared context management, not identical implementations (OASC, 2024) — sharing insight without sharing a single record.

### 4.4 The hospital of 2040: AEST at the bedside [target]

Zoom to the ward; anchor on the patient. In SPIN terms the ward is precisely SMILE's canonical Place — the hospital ward that is also a boundary of jurisdiction (Waern, 2026) — where Space is the measured thermal, spatial, and physiological environment, Interaction is the flow between clinicians, sensors, and the twin, and Network is the federation of ward twins, patient twins, and population models the anchor sits inside. At that anchor, all four AEST operations activate. **Absorb**: the twin ingests the patient's recorded trajectory, not a discharge-summary abstraction. **Emulate**: the living present-state twin tracks the patient as she is now, the way TREWS already tracks the septic prodrome hours ahead of clinical recognition. **Simulate**: interventions are rehearsed in silico before they touch the body — Trayanova's virtual VT induction is this operation running in 2025, at pilot scale. **Transcend**: clinician and patient define the desired outcome state — discharged, ambulatory, off the drug — and the twin reverse-engineers the care path from that future back to today's order set. Simulation here is not prediction; it is aspiration made actionable (Waern, 2026).

Two negotiations distinguish 2040 from 2026. First, the ward twin and the patient twin negotiate directly — bed capacity against clinical trajectory, staffing against acuity — the intent-based delegation of Section 6.3 applied to care. Second, at the hybrid quantum-classical (HQC) stage of the DTC maturity path, this paper projects [projection] that personalized dosing could become a quantum-chemistry problem: variational eigensolvers of the lineage running from photonic proof-of-concept (Peruzzo et al., 2014) through vendor-reported hybrid reaction simulations [demonstrated, not peer-reviewed] (IonQ, 2025) might one day compute binding behavior against a patient's own variant profile. This is a projection, not a near-term deliverable: there is today no evidence that variational eigensolvers can compute clinically actionable per-patient binding at any near horizon. The benchmark gate is the discipline that keeps the projection honest: quantum dosing would ship only if it beats the classical pharmacokinetic baseline on the same patient data, and not one day earlier [target].

### 4.5 The hospital that never forgets a lesson

Phase 6, Perpetual Wisdom, is the endpoint that repairs the past hospital's amnesia. The retired charge nurse's eight-hour instinct is not lost when she leaves; it is absorbed into the ward twin's ontology, where five years of confirmed alerts, rehearsed interventions, and outcome trajectories compound into a node measurably wiser than any single clinician — Waern's (2026) formulation that time compounds knowledge in a SMILE deployment rather than decaying it, which this paper extends to perpetual reality recording. Reality is the primary construct — perpetually recorded, sovereign, corrigible — and every model, alert, and dosing plan is a secondary instrument answerable to it. That is the whole inversion: the past hospital produced documents about care and let the wisdom evaporate; the 2040 hospital produces care and lets nothing evaporate at all.

## 5. Precision Manufacturing and Life Sciences: Yield, Molecules, and the Real-Fictional Dynamic

The city chapters measure autonomy in control loops; the factory and the clinic measure it in yield and mortality. These two domains matter to a quantum-city argument for a precise reason: they are where the twin-first pattern has produced its hardest numbers, and where SMILE's real-fictional dynamic — a completely simulated future that has never existed can be emulated by reality, and real reality can be emulated digitally (Waern, 2026) — has stopped being a philosophical claim and become an operating procedure. What a smart building learns from a semiconductor fab is not the process recipe. It is the discipline: the fictional (simulated) future is built first, interrogated at computational speed, and only then permitted to instruct the real present.

### 5.1 Manufacturing today: measured, not projected

The manufacturing evidence base is unusually clean because the baseline is unforgiving — a fab or an assembly line has a costed downtime minute and a counted defect. Siemens reports that IoT-fed 3D twins of gas turbines cut unexpected breakdowns by 20% and extended operational life by 10%, with digital-twin adopters seeing up to 30% operational-cost reduction and, in some facilities, 50% defect-rate reduction [deployed for the measured breakdown/life figures; the "up to 30%" and "up to 50%" bands are vendor modeling ranges, projection] (Siemens AG, 2024). A leading semiconductor manufacturer applying twin simulation to yield engineering recorded a 25% increase in first-time-right designs, freed 20% of engineering capacity, and shortened root-cause-analysis cycles by 30% [demonstrated]; McKinsey's fab-wide modeling puts AI/ML potential at 28–32% R&D cost reduction and 13–17% off cost of goods sold [projection] (McKinsey & Company, 2023). BMW's iFACTORY virtual factories — OpenUSD twins spanning more than one million square meters, in which planners optimize layout, robotics, and logistics years before production and run automated collision checks ahead of launch — carry a projected 30% reduction in production-planning cost [deployed twin, projected saving] (BMW Group, 2025).

The BMW case is the purest industrial expression of SMILE Phase 2, which validates interventions virtually before they are executed physically (Waern, 2026), and of the concurrent-engineering practice from major space agencies on which SMILE's concurrent phases explicitly draw: every discipline designs against the same shared model simultaneously, so integration errors surface in the fictional plant while they still cost minutes rather than months. AEST's Simulate operation is running here on an anchor point that does not yet exist — the factory of three years hence — and Transcend then reverse-engineers the buildable path from that validated future back to today's purchase orders. This is Grieves's front-running simulation (replicate, predict, navigate) applied to capital allocation rather than fault management (Grieves, 2023).

Quantum's entry point into this stack is already operational at the quantum-inspired (QI) rung of the DTC maturity ladder. Fujitsu's Digital Annealer has been deployed in Toyota production-planning trials [deployed] (Fujitsu Limited & Toyota Systems Corporation, 2022) — the same maturity stage, and the same QUBO formulation, as the scheduling problems Chapter 6 assigned to city networks. The through-line matters more than the hardware: production sequencing, changeover rehearsal for lights-out operation, and multi-line portfolio scheduling are all quadratic assignment problems. Today they run on CMOS annealers benchmarked competitive with specialized classical solvers on dense instances (Aramon et al., 2019); under the benchmark gate defined earlier, a QPU replaces the annealer only on the day it wins on wall-clock and cost — and the twin's data model does not change when it does. A lights-out factory in 2035 is, in this framing, a Phase 5 SMILE deployment: intelligence at the physical edge, every changeover rehearsed in the fictional plant before the real line reconfigures itself unattended.

### 5.2 Life sciences: from structure prediction to the virtual control arm

Where manufacturing disciplines matter, life sciences disciplines molecules and mortality — and the twin pattern reaches further up the value chain. Because the vendor's headline speedup figure lacks a stated baseline, hardware, and problem size, this paper does not carry a numeric multiplier for it.

The clinical twin is where the real-fictional dynamic becomes ethically load-bearing. ## 6. The Operator: ARPU, Churn, and the Economics of Networks That Heal Themselves

### 6.1 The revenue floor is flat and the cost floor is not

Strip away the technology narrative and the operator's position in 2026 reduces to three numbers. First, revenue: GSMA Intelligence projects mobile operator revenue growing at roughly 1.8% CAGR from 2023 to 2030, reaching approximately $1.3 trillion — subscriber growth is saturating while ARPU declines (GSMA Intelligence, 2025) [deployed]. Services beyond core connectivity already reached 22% of major operators' total revenue in 2019, up from 17% in 2017 (GSMA Intelligence, 2020) [deployed] — connectivity alone stopped carrying operator economics before 5G was widely deployed. Second, the 5G disappointment: early projections of 16–20% ARPU uplift largely failed to materialize; mobile revenue barely tracked inflation despite ~2.8 billion 5G connections, because the infrastructure was built before monetizable use cases existed [industry-estimate] (Teral Research, 2025). Only ~20% of 5G operators have deployed the Standalone architecture that slicing requires (GSA/Opensignal, 2026) [deployed], while consumer connectivity still accounts for roughly 70% of operator *revenue* (GSMA Intelligence, 2025) [deployed]. The one clearly working new revenue line is prosaic: fixed wireless access accounts for an estimated 20–25% of recent operator revenue growth, with connections forecast to grow toward roughly 330–350 million by 2030 (Ericsson, 2025) [deployed]. Third, the cost side, where the numbers are anything but flat. Energy can represent up to ~40% of network opex in high-energy-cost markets, and the RAN consumes roughly 73% of network energy (GSMA, n.d.) [deployed]. When the network fails, it fails expensively, at the magnitudes Chapter 2 documents — including the February 2024 AT&T outage, the reference case for what autonomy exists to prevent (Federal Communications Commission, 2024) [demonstrated].

Churn converts network quality directly into revenue leakage. Postpaid churn benchmarks run 0.75–3% per month, prepaid 3–5% (STL Partners, n.d.) [demonstrated]; the industry averages roughly 22% annually with among the lowest NPS of any sector (CustomerGauge, 2025) [demonstrated]. Network quality is consistently among the top two or three drivers of smartphone-user churn [industry-estimate] (Vecta Labs, n.d.). The arithmetic is clarifying but must be read as gross revenue at risk, not margin: for a 30-million-subscriber carrier at $55 blended ARPU (a US-postpaid-typical figure; European and Asian blended ARPU of $15–35 would scale this down 2–3×), each percentage point of churn puts roughly $198 million per year of *gross revenue* at risk. The P&L impact is far smaller — churned subscribers are largely replaced by gross adds, so the real cost is net churn plus the acquisition-cost differential, adjusted to EBITDA margin (roughly 35–45% of the gross figure). Replacing a churned subscriber costs more than retaining one — US-postpaid CAC with device subsidy commonly runs several hundred dollars against a lower retention-offer cost — a differential in the 6–10× range [demonstrated, range disputed] (industry estimates; ChurnCost, 2026). An operator that cannot grow the top line must defend it, and the defense runs through the network's ability to notice, predict, and repair itself.

### 6.2 The autonomy ladder, audited

TM Forum's L0–L5 autonomous-network levels are the instrument that turns "self-healing" from adjective into audit finding. The levels are defined in IG1218 (AN Business Requirements & Framework), evaluated per the IG1252 levels-evaluation methodology, and realized architecturally through the Open Digital Architecture (ODA) and the IG1230 AN reference architecture, with TMF921 as the intent-management API within ODA (TM Forum, 2024; document versions to be verified against current releases before publication). The audited reality in early 2026: Rakuten Mobile holds TM Forum-validated Level 4 autonomy for one use case — RAN energy efficiency, scenario GB1059H — in a live commercial Open RAN network, with AI closed-loop control on production traffic delivering approximately 20% RAN energy savings and no measured customer-experience impact (Rakuten Mobile, 2026) [deployed, vendor self-report, TM Forum-validated]. Operators including Telefónica report multiple L4 use cases in scoped domains [operator-reported, unaudited by author] (primary source to be confirmed). China Mobile, operating near-L4 in IP backhaul and RAN fault management, reports an 80% reduction in major faults and more than 3,200 person-years of effort saved; a TM Forum survey finds 20% of operators reporting L4/L5 in select domains, 35% expecting it within two years, and — the load-bearing figure — L4/L5 capabilities contributing roughly 30% of the total financial benefit of autonomy (Fierce Network, 2025) [demonstrated]. Bain's independent reality check belongs beside these claims: most operators remain at L2–L3, and realized opex savings lag vendor projections (Bain & Company, 2024) [demonstrated]. McKinsey's modeling — up to 30% IT cost reduction, 10–15% ARPU uplift, 10–15% capex optimization from end-to-end AI industrialization — is consultant arithmetic, not observed P&L (McKinsey & Company, 2025) [projection].

Set the 20% Rakuten figure against the cost structure above: 20% of the energy consumed by the component that draws roughly 73% of network energy, in a line item that can reach ~40% of opex, is a margin intervention measured in whole percentage points — from a single certified use case.

### 6.3 The economic chain: from prediction to basis points

The value of the self-healing network resolves into three chains, each with a different clock.

**Prediction → degradation avoided → churn basis points.** If network quality is among the leading churn drivers, and the front-running twin — replicate, predict, navigate, in Grieves's (2023) formulation — screens degradations before subscribers experience them, then every avoided quality event is a retention intervention purchased at the lower retention price rather than the higher replacement price. Deriving the value properly — as avoided replacements × CAC differential, plus margin on retained revenue, rather than full ARPU — shaving even 20–50 basis points off annual churn plausibly yields a mid-eight-figure annuity for a mid-sized carrier [projection, derived from the churn-economics and network-quality estimates above, which are industry figures not primary data]. This is where SMILE's NUDEDA discipline bites: the methodology proceeds from identified need toward data requirements rather than the dominant reverse direction (Waern, 2026). The need is retention basis points; the data requirement — which counters to collect, which cells to twin — derives from it, not the reverse.

**Optimization → efficiency → margin.** The Rakuten energy loop is the audited instance; spectral-efficiency and capacity-deferral loops follow the same pattern. These monetize inside the existing P&L and require no new customer.

**Twin-mediated services → new ARPU lines.** This is the speculative chain, and honesty about its status is a design input. Network slicing forecasts diverge by more than 20×: ABI Research projects $67.5 billion by 2030, while MarketsandMarkets projects roughly $2.81 billion for the same year, and standardized northbound APIs have seen little adoption [roadmap; the divergence is itself the citable fact] (ABI Research, 2025; MarketsandMarkets, 2025). What makes a slice sellable to an enterprise is not bandwidth but a verifiable SLA — an intent expressed through TMF921, monitored by the twin, with the audit trail as the product. Integrated sensing and communication extends the same logic: dense cellular infrastructure becomes a "perceptive network" in which communication and sensing converge in hardware, channel, and signal processing (Liu et al., 2022) [peer-reviewed-theory], turning the operator's densest asset — sites — into sensing-as-a-service inventory. Both offerings are, in SMILE terms, the network twin acting as boundary object between operator and enterprise customer: the SLA dispute is settled by querying the shared, provenance-signed record — retrieval over generation, the Crucible constraint applied to billing.

At the asymptote sits a design target this paper labels explicitly: driving the handover-failure rate asymptotically toward the radio-physics floor [target] — not a "100%" marketing number. Handover success is already 99.5–99.9% in well-run networks, and residual failures are dominated by radio physics (deep fades, interference bursts) and UE behavior that no pre-simulation removes; per-event rehearsal is not credible at X2/Xn timescales and network scale. The realistic target is therefore zero mobility-*caused* perceptible failures, achieved by the twin optimizing handover policies and parameters — an evolved mobility-robustness-optimization loop — rather than rehearsing individual handovers. This is AEST's Transcend operation applied to radio operations: reverse-engineering the path from the desired future state back to present parameter-tuning action.

### 6.4 Where quantum enters the operator's P&L

Two insertion points survive the benchmark gate. First, alarm correlation inside collapsing decision windows: as autonomy compresses fault-response loops from hours toward seconds, the combinatorial problem of correlating alarm storms across domains grows while the time to solve it shrinks — quantum-inspired solvers (QI on the DTC Quantum WG ladder) are deployable now behind an abstracted solver interface. Second, portfolio-scale placement — sites, spectrum, slices, energy contracts optimized jointly — where hybrid quantum-classical solvers (HQC) enter only if and when they beat the classical baseline, per the working group's gate: quantum ships only when it wins the benchmark. The operator's twin, recording reality perpetually per the Phase 6 extension, is what makes both the benchmark and the business case computable — the knowledge that a solver earned its place persists beyond the project that proved it.

## 7. Densification to Holography: The 6G Physical Layer of a Holographic Society

### 7.1 What Densification Actually Buys

The 2021 vision literature framed densification as a capacity story: cell shrinkage at higher bands as the price of terahertz rates (Tataria et al., 2021). The more consequential reading, now anchored in peer-reviewed theory, is that densification buys *perception*. Liu et al. (2022) show that dense cellular infrastructure — mmWave apertures plus massive MIMO — makes communication and sensing converge in hardware, channel, and signal-processing terms, turning the deployment grid into a "perceptive network" [peer-reviewed-theory]. ITU-R M.2160-0 canonizes this as integrated sensing and communication (ISAC), one of six IMT-2030 usage scenarios, with capability ranges stated explicitly as design targets, not achieved performance (International Telecommunication Union, 2023) [target]. The contrast case is instructive: Huawei (2022) claims centimeter-level outdoor localization via wider bandwidth and larger apertures — a lab-condition prototype result that should be read against the ITU-R framework, not alongside it [projection].

How dense, and who pays? Related deployment literature puts roughly 60 base stations/km² as the requirement for 0.95 coverage probability at 30 GHz, with wireless line-of-sight fronthaul at mmWave/sub-THz demonstrated as the mechanism that removes the fiber bottleneck dominating small-cell economics (IEEE, 2024) [demonstrated]. GSMA Intelligence (2021) prices the reality check: $2,000–5,000 per small cell in equipment and up to ~$20,000 per site fully loaded with permitting, power, and haul [projection — industry estimate]. Two escape routes soften the bill. First, the economically favored capacity path runs through centimetric 7–15 GHz on *existing* macro grids, with sub-THz relegated to hotspot spectrum — a documented industry-research counterweight to "10× density everywhere" narratives [preprint] (arXiv:2406.18391, 2024). Second, reconfigurable intelligent surfaces programmably reshape propagation to fill coverage holes at a fraction of a new site's cost [preprint] (arXiv:2506.19526, 2025) — though the industry R&D consortium view is blunter: RIS trials demonstrate mmWave hole-filling, but cost, control-channel overhead, and ETSI standardization remain open before RIS substitutes for buildout (IEEE Communications Magazine, 2024) [demonstrated].

### 7.2 The Holographic Requirement and the Honest Gap

The ITU-T Focus Group on Network 2030 defined holographic-type communications (HTC) as a compound service: very high bandwidth, multi-sense coordinated stream synchronization, and sub-20 ms latency, listed among seven representative use cases alongside the digital twin and the tactile Internet (ITU-T FG NET-2030, 2020) [target]. Peer-reviewed synthesis sharpens the numbers: human-scale light-field holograms require roughly 100 Gbps–2 Tbps depending on compression — raw uncompressed estimates reach 4.3–6.6 Tbps at 40 fps — with sub-millisecond motion-to-photon budgets (Taleb et al., 2022) [peer-reviewed-theory].

Now the audit. IMT-2030 peak-rate targets sit orders of magnitude below the upper HTC envelope (International Telecommunication Union, 2023). Ericsson's (2025) own traffic forecast — ~328 EB/month by 2031, with XR explicitly *unmodeled* in the base case — concedes that holographic-scale traffic is roadmap, not forecast, even in vendor numbers [projection]. And the sensing side has its own binding constraint: constructing holographic digital twins from multi-base-station ISAC fusion requires unified spatial coordinate systems and synchronized sampling across sites, with receiver position/time synchronization identified as the limiting factor even in the most practical mid-end fusion architectures [preprint] (arXiv:2502.13352, 2025). The honest statement: full-fidelity HTC violates no physics, but it exceeds every standardized 6G capability target, and the synchronization problem is harder than the bandwidth problem.

This is why the digital twin is not merely a 6G *application* but its design and operations instrument. GPU ray-tracing RAN twins already run site-specific, city-scale simulations — hundreds of base stations, thousands of devices — as the training-and-validation loop for placement and algorithms before live deployment (NVIDIA, 2024) [deployed]. A densified network too complex to plan on spreadsheets gets planned inside its own twin; the Hexa-X flagship's "massive twinning" use-case family and its Key Value Indicator method — tying technical KPIs to societal value rather than data-rate-first hype — supply the peer-reviewed framing for that inversion (Uusitalo et al., 2021) [peer-reviewed-theory]. This is NUDEDA discipline applied to spectrum: proceed from identified need toward density requirements, never density-first.

### 7.3 The Holographic Society: The Boundary Object Made Sensory

SMILE Phase 6 — Perpetual Wisdom — names its aspirational endpoint the *holographic society*, and the term must be read precisely. It is not teleportation theater. SMILE's core thesis holds that reality digitally rendered is "the ultimate boundary object": the one referent all communities share regardless of vocabulary (Waern, 2026). This paper's extension of Phase 6 to perpetual reality recording establishes reality as the primary construct; every dashboard, model, and hologram is a secondary, corrigible instrument rendered from it.

## 8. Interoperability Is the Constitution: MIMs, Boundary Objects, and Governance That Survives

### 8.1 Minimal Mechanisms, Not Maximal Standards

Constitutions endure because they specify little. They fix the mechanisms of coexistence — who may decide what, how disputes resolve, what rights are non-negotiable — and leave everything else to the communities living under them. The Open & Agile Smart Cities Minimal Interoperability Mechanisms embody the same insight for cities: no two municipalities will run identical stacks, so the only agreement that survives procurement cycles, vendor turnover, and political change is agreement on the *minimum* mechanisms two systems need to exchange meaningful information. The MIMs, by their official short names, are MIM1 Context, MIM2 Data Models, MIM3 Contracts, MIM4 Trust/Personal Data Management, MIM5 Transparency (fair AI and algorithmic transparency), MIM6 Security, and MIM7 Places (geospatial), with further MIMs in development (Open & Agile Smart Cities & Living-in.EU Technical Sub-Group, 2024) [deployed]. More than 150 sub-national governments have committed to the MIMs through OASC and the Living-in.EU declaration — whose MIMs Plus profile extends them — though commitment is not the same as demonstrated mutual interoperation, which remains far narrower. Chapter 6 introduced this as an architectural property; this chapter reads it as a governance instrument, because that is what it actually is. A minimal mechanism is a constitutional clause: hard to change, deliberately sparse, and binding on implementations it never anticipated. MIM7 Places is worth flagging in a paper about spatial twins: it is the MIM most directly aligned with SPIN's Place dimension — the machine-readable geospatial referent on which humanly meaningful interpretation hangs.

MIM1, context information management, deserves the closest reading because it is the clause everything else depends on. Context requires an anchor: a starting point — an entity, event, or location — without which context becomes vague and undefined for lack of a frame of reference (Waern, 2026). Actor-Network Theory supplies the anchor's theoretical grammar: sensors, people, and institutions are all actors in sociotechnological ecosystems, co-creating the network's dynamics rather than passively occupying it, so MIM1-style context management must acknowledge the active role of those actors and their interrelationships in how information is collected, shared, and used (Waern, 2026). The practical corollary is the boundary box: to think outside the box, one must define the box first. A context broker that cannot state whose reality it brokers, at what spatial and temporal extent, has not defined its boundary box — and every downstream governance question (whose consent? whose liability? whose energy bill?) inherits the vagueness. Federated implementations of exactly this pattern exist: NGSI-LD context brokers under a Gaia-X trust framework integrating transport, energy, water, and lighting across a live city platform (Cirillo et al., 2024) [demonstrated].

### 8.2 Reality as the Boundary-Spanning Object

Minimal mechanisms solve the machine-to-machine problem. The harder problem is human: the communities that must coordinate around city infrastructure share no vocabulary. SMILE's answer is that reality itself — not a model of reality, but reality digitally rendered — is the ultimate boundary object, because physical reality is the one thing all communities share regardless of disciplinary vocabulary, institutional affiliation, or cultural background (Waern, 2026). A SMILE-produced twin functions as a coincident boundary object: the RF engineer reads electromagnetic propagation from the scene, the structural engineer reads load distribution, the facility manager reads energy flows — all reading the same scene, none required to abandon their domain language. The SPIN dimensions make this operational: Space is the objective, measurable physical environment that does not care about human interpretation; Place is its humanly meaningful reading — the hospital ward that is also a jurisdiction, the district that is also a political unit; Interaction is the four-channel exchange (From People, From Systems, From Planet, From AI); Network is the ecosystem of interconnected twins in which any single twin is a node (Waern, 2026). In this framing MIM1 is the machine-readable half of Place: the negotiated shared meaning that different communities attach to a common spatial referent, encoded so an AI agent can join the negotiation. When the nurse, the grid engineer, and the autonomous agent coordinate a hospital's response to a feeder fault, they do not agree on an ontology; they point at the same rendered reality and each takes the action their community authorizes — the intent interface (TMF921, within ODA's intent-management function) doing for the agent what the ward round does for the nurse (TM Forum, 2024) [demonstrated].

The ordering discipline matters as much as the object. NUDEDA — proceed from identified Need through Utility, Design, Development, to Adoption — inverts the dominant data-first practice (Waern, 2026), and the cost of ignoring it is the one Chapter 2 quantifies. A city that pools its data into a lake before defining the need has built an expensive liability, not a constitution.

### 8.3 Governance for Perpetual Recording

This paper's extension of SMILE Phase 6 commits the city to something no prior infrastructure generation attempted: perpetual reality recording — knowledge that persists beyond project closure and outlives the teams that created it. A perpetual record of urban reality is constitutionally different from a sensor feed. It demands rules of constitutional grade: who may query which slice of reality, at which zoom level, over which temporal window — because AEST operations make the archive traversable, and each operation carries distinct governance weight. An Absorb query into ten years of occupancy traces is a surveillance act; a Transcend query is a political one, since it encodes whose desired future the city reverse-engineers toward. Sidewalk Toronto is the canonical failure to answer these questions in advance: the proposed Urban Data Trust lacked clarity on the problem it solved, its accountability, and its relation to existing data-protection law, and the project was cancelled in 2020 amid COVID-era uncertainty after two years of governance controversy that had already cost it its Privacy-by-Design advisers (Goodman & Powles, 2019) [demonstrated]. Two clauses follow. First, explainability as a civil right: every automated decision drawn from the record must pass a Crucible-style gate — retrieval over generation, provenance on every data point, no output that cannot be explained in domain language to a non-technical stakeholder (Waern, 2026). Second, differential privacy as protocol, not promise: mathematical guarantees that individuals cannot be reverse-engineered from aggregate insights, one of the six convergent capabilities of the holographic-society endpoint (Waern, 2026) [target].

Perpetual recording also creates an archival-ethics obligation that ordinary security policy misses: protecting the recorded past from the future. Adversaries are harvesting ciphertext now to decrypt when cryptographically relevant quantum computers arrive (National Security Agency, 2021) [deployed], and given 10–15-year quantum timelines against 5–10-year migration cycles, long-lived data intercepted today is already exposed (Ott et al., 2025). A perpetual archive with a fifty-year confidentiality horizon must therefore adopt the finalized NIST post-quantum standards (National Institute of Standards and Technology, 2024) [deployed] at the archive layer first — not because the archive is the most attacked surface, but because it is the longest-lived one.

The final constitutional test is degraded-mode sovereignty. The concentration and lock-in failures Chapter 2 documents — an 8.5-million-device crash, region-wide cascades, punitive egress — mean a city whose twin dies with its cloud tenancy has signed away sovereignty in a service agreement. The constitutional clause — edge-native, local-first, cloud by exception, data sovereignty as a hardware property rather than a policy checkbox (Waern, 2026) — is testable: partition the city from its cloud, as a solar storm or regional outage will eventually do, and observe whether heating still regulates, intents still resolve locally, and the reality record still accrues. Interoperability that survives that test is a constitution. Everything else is a vendor contract with civic branding.

## 9. Conclusion: Decisions That Persist

Strip away the technology inventory and this whitepaper reduces to five architectural decisions, each of which a city signs — usually inside a procurement annex nobody reads twice — and each of which compounds for decades.

**Edge-native versus cloud-first.** The centralized path has produced its own evidence file, quantified in Chapter 2: single-point faults crashing millions of devices, region-wide cascades, and egress tolls that turn leaving into a five-figure decision per 50 TB, now under EU antitrust scrutiny. SMILE Phase 5's edge-native inference — local actuation first, sovereignty-preserving synchronization later — is not an ideology; it is the architecture whose failure modes are local rather than civilizational (Waern, 2026).

**Need-first versus data-first.** The NUDEDA lens is blunt: proceed from identified NEED toward data requirements, never data-first (Waern, 2026). The data-first counterfactual carries the price tag Chapter 2 details. Regression-based planning adds an epistemic ceiling on top of the quality problem: forecasts deteriorate sharply under structural breaks (Xu & Perron, 2019), and distinct stochastic models of complex systems become statistically indistinguishable from finite data [preprint] (arXiv:2509.24977, 2025) — the formal case for Grieves's front-running simulation over curve-fitting (Grieves, 2023).

**Minimal interoperability versus vendor lock.** The MIMs premise — cities interoperate through minimal mechanisms, not identical implementations — is the only interoperability model that survives contact with municipal procurement cycles. Its negation was field-tested in Toronto: Sidewalk Labs's centralized Urban Data Trust was cancelled in 2020 amid COVID-era uncertainty after two years of governance and privacy controversy (Goodman & Powles, 2019) [demonstrated].

**Post-quantum cryptography now versus later.** A city that records its reality perpetually creates the longest-lived ciphertext in existence, and harvest-now-decrypt-later means that data is already being collected against future decryption [deployed] (National Security Agency, 2021). With 10–15-year quantum timelines against 5–10-year migration cycles, the arithmetic closes before the threat materializes (Ott et al., 2025); CNSA 2.0 (National Security Agency, 2022) mandates a phased transition beginning 2025–2027 by system category, with exclusive CNSA 2.0 use required across national security systems by 2033. Perpetual recordings signed with legacy cryptography are liabilities with century-scale duration.

**Perpetual wisdom versus project amnesia.** SMILE Phase 6 makes knowledge that persists beyond project closure the exit condition of any serious deployment, because time compounds knowledge in a SMILE deployment rather than decaying it (Waern, 2026). This paper extends that commitment to perpetual reality recording. The alternative is the default: twins archived at handover, institutional memory walking out with the integrator.

Three research items follow directly, suited to joint DTC–TM Forum–OASC execution. First, the solver-neutral problem-interchange schema and PQC twin profiles proposed above need reference implementations against a live testbed, with the working group's benchmark gate — quantum ships only if it beats the classical baseline — as the acceptance criterion. Second, benchmark and source-quality disclosure should become a MIM-class mechanism itself: every autonomy or optimization claim labeled deployed, demonstrated, or projection, exactly as the >20× divergence in 2030 slicing forecasts ($67.5B versus $2.81B) shows is necessary [roadmap] (ABI Research, 2025; MarketsandMarkets, 2025). Third, action-labeled world datasets: today's city data records states, not interventions-with-outcomes. AEST's Transcend operation — reverse-engineering the path from the desired sustainable future back to present action (Waern, 2026) — is uncomputable without recorded (state, action, outcome) triples. Building that corpus is the single highest-leverage data investment a city can make.

The closing argument belongs to time. This paper's organizing claim is that reality is the primary construct — the perpetual recording against which everything else is a secondary, corrigible instrument. A model can be retrained; a standard can be revised; a vendor can be replaced. A gap in the recording of what the city actually was, and what was actually done, in 2026, cannot be repaired in 2046. A city that commits to perpetual recording is not buying software; its ontology and interoperability choices will persist across the fifty-year lifecycle of the assets they govern. That commitment is being made now, in procurement language, by people who mostly believe they are buying a network upgrade — and the purpose of this whitepaper is to make sure they know what they are actually signing: decisions built to outlast every institution now at the table.
