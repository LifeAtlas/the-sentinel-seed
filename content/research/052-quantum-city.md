## Abstract

Infrastructure commissioned today will operate for decades, yet the systems that observe and protect it — dashboards, data lakes, cryptography — are refreshed on far shorter cycles, and organizations increasingly trust the readout over the reality it represents. This paper argues that reality must be treated as the primary, perpetually-recorded, boundary-spanning construct across the operators, cities, and services that share it, and that the decisions determining whether its recording is sovereign, explainable, and quantum-safe are being made now. Drawing on the wartime resilience of Ukraine's networks, it reframes the central design question away from cloud versus on-premise toward reachability under partition: no data or control loop should depend on a single reachable location. The empirical anchor is an Open RAN self-healing case from a dense-urban Tokyo cluster (75 cells; 12,819,049 handover attempts), in which closed-loop Cell Individual Offset tuning lifted problem-cell handover success from approximately 84% to approximately 92%. The paper specifies its method through SMILE (Sustainable Methodology for Interoperable Lifecycle Enablement) and maps self-healing to the TM Forum Autonomous Networks levels; models the operator economics of ARPU and churn; examines resilience through capturing tacit human skill as robots offset workforce shortages; traces the reality-capture stack from Gaussian splatting through world-action models, the spatial web, and sovereign identity; treats post-quantum cryptography as archival ethics; positions Minimal Interoperability Mechanisms as constitutional infrastructure; and closes with a practitioner action set. A single falsifiable criterion — the partition test — governs throughout: a system that cannot keep reality readable when its network and power are lost was never sovereign. Every factual claim carries an explicit evidence tier.

## 1. Greenland Was Always the Biggest

Ask a room full of clever people the oldest trick question in geography: *What was the biggest island in the world before Greenland was discovered?* The confident answers come fast — Australia is a continent, so it doesn't count; maybe New Guinea, maybe Borneo. The answer is Greenland. It was always Greenland. The island did not shrink because no one had charted it, and it did not swell to its true size the day a cartographer finally inked its coastline. Its reality never waited on our map.

Here is the twist that makes the joke worth telling in a paper about infrastructure. The map most of us grew up with — the Mercator projection tacked to a thousand classroom walls — draws Greenland as a white giant, sprawling as wide as Africa. Yet Africa is roughly fourteen times larger. The projection stretches the far north to keep sailing lines straight, and in doing so it distorts size. The tool distorted; the land never moved. Greenland is precisely as big as it has always been; only our instrument for looking at it was wrong. The error lived entirely in the readout, never in the reality.

Now the leap a bright ten-year-old can follow. A little over a century ago, Einstein worked out something that sounds like a magic trick but is plain, tested fact (Einstein, 1905). Two people moving differently — one standing on a platform, one racing past on a train — will honestly read different times on their watches and different lengths on their rulers. They will disagree about how long something took and how far apart two things are. But they are not disagreeing about *what happened*. The event happened once. A lightning strike struck where it struck, when it struck. The two observers simply hold up different instruments, and their instruments give different numbers. Later, Einstein extended the same lesson to gravity and space itself (Einstein, 1916). The clocks differ; the rulers differ; the event is the event no matter whose watch you trust. Readings differ. Reality is one.

That is the thesis of this paper, compressed into a child's riddle and a physicist's insight: **the readout is not the reality.** Trusting the dashboard over the thing the dashboard measures, trusting the data lake over the water it claims to model, trusting the digital twin over the machine it mirrors — that is the same mistake as believing Greenland is the size Mercator drew it. It is an inversion. We have quietly let the instruments of observation outrank the observed. We patch the map instead of walking the land. At civilizational scale, that inversion is not merely a philosophy-seminar curiosity. It is expensive, and increasingly it is dangerous.

For evidence that the stakes are real, leave the classroom and go to Kyiv.

When Russia's invasion began in February 2022, telecommunications engineers faced a scenario that Western network planners had spent decades treating as unthinkable: sustained physical destruction, cyberattack, and grid collapse, simultaneously, indefinitely. Ukrainian operators stood up national roaming within roughly a week, so a subscriber whose home network was cratered could latch onto whichever competitor's radio still had signal. As Russian strikes dismantled the power grid through late 2022, telecom sites rode out an estimated 65% loss of grid power on batteries, generators, and improvisation, and the network stayed reachable [demonstrated] (secondary reporting; ITU, 2023). The state had already seen the shape of this coming: an emergency law passed in the invasion's opening days dissolved the old requirement that government data sit on servers inside national borders, and Ukraine dispersed its institutional record to cloud infrastructure beyond the reach of any single missile [deployed] (Government of Ukraine, 2022). The record of the state was decoupled from any one place it could be destroyed. These two figures — roughly 65% grid-power loss withstood, national roaming within a week — are stated here once and cross-referenced by name in later chapters rather than repeated in full.

The adversary understood the target precisely. In December 2023, the Sandworm unit executed a wiper attack on Kyivstar, Ukraine's largest operator, corrupting core systems and severing service for roughly 24 million subscribers [demonstrated] (CERT-UA, 2023). That single fact demolishes a comfortable assumption: the cloud is not automatically safe, because a sufficiently determined attacker who reaches the control plane can erase what lives there as thoroughly as any bomb. And the celebrated workaround carried its own warning. Starlink kept units connected when nothing else could, but a capability that depends on one vendor's goodwill and one founder's disposition is a chokepoint wearing the costume of resilience.

Strip Kyiv down to its engineering lesson and the debate changes shape. The question that matters is not *cloud versus on-premises* — that framing is a Mercator distortion of its own. Both a cloud and a local server are single places, and a single place can be cut off, bombed, or wiped. The real axis is **reachability under partition**: when the network fragments and the power dies, can the people who need the readable model of reality still reach it? The design principle that falls out is blunt enough to carry through every chapter that follows: *no data should depend on one reachable place.*

This is the unit of contribution this paper offers, stated once here and defended thereafter: **reality is the primary, perpetually-recorded, boundary-spanning construct, and the architecture decisions that make its recording sovereign, explainable, and quantum-safe are being locked in now.** The twin is not the machine. The dashboard is not the network. But the recording of reality — if we engineer it to survive partition, to explain itself, and to resist the cryptographic threats already on the horizon — becomes the one thing an institution can trust when every instrument disagrees.

Because a claim you cannot break is not engineering but faith, this paper binds itself to a single falsifier, applied without exception from here forward — **the partition test:** *does the readable model of reality survive the simultaneous loss of network and power?* If it does not, the architecture has failed, no matter how elegant its dashboard. Every design choice in the chapters ahead earns its place by passing that test or is discarded.

The remainder proceeds in four movements: the full cost of the inversion, which runs deeper than one war zone reveals; what becomes possible when reality itself is engineered to last fifty years, and its economics; and finally the concrete decisions worth making now. Greenland was always the biggest island. The only open question is whether our instruments are honest enough to admit it.

## 2. The Comfortable Catastrophe: Why Today Cannot Hold

The dangerous thing about the systems most operators run today is that they work. The lights stay on. The dashboards stay green. The quarterly report closes. Comfort is exactly the problem: an infrastructure can be profitable, audited, and fully staffed while resting on foundations that cannot survive the first serious shock. This chapter surfaces the situation an operator actually runs, names the problems inside it, and traces the implication that makes those problems compound over a fifty-year asset life. The catastrophe is not that today fails loudly. It is that today succeeds quietly, right up until it doesn't.

**The situation: a business run on artifacts, not reality.** Start with the tool most critical decisions still pass through — the spreadsheet. This is not a strawman. Operations, capacity planning, and reconciliation across telecom and utilities remain spreadsheet-mediated, and the error literature is unambiguous: careful audits find that the large majority of operational spreadsheets contain errors, with cell error rates measured at roughly 1–5% and error incidence rising with sheet size (Panko, 2008) [demonstrated]. A model that is wrong in one cell out of fifty is not a model of reality; it is a curve fit to whatever survived the last copy-paste. When that artifact becomes the source of truth for how many spares to stock or how much load a feeder can carry, the organization is navigating by a chart it knows to be smudged and refuses to redraw.

**The problem sharpens when the underlying data has holes.** Planning routinely runs on incomplete records stitched together with nonseasonal assumptions — annualized averages standing in for demand that is violently seasonal, sensor gaps interpolated as though the missing hours resembled the present ones. This is statistics in the oldest sense: fitting a smooth curve to a dead past and extrapolating it forward as if the future were obligated to comply. It is the opposite of what a live system needs, which is simulation on an emulated present-state model — a twin that reflects the plant as it *is* now, then runs scenarios forward. The distinction is the difference between explaining yesterday and surviving tomorrow. An operator who can only describe the past has, in effect, chosen the Mercator projection: a readout that is internally consistent and comprehensively wrong about proportion.

**The implication is that these errors do not stay small — they capitalize.** A wrong assumption baked into a fifty-year procurement decision does not cost once; it accrues interest every year the asset is in the ground. A data hole in 2026 becomes a stranded architecture in 2046. And the compounding is not merely economic. It is architectural, because the second structural problem in today's stack is *where the data goes to be processed.*

**Data that leaves to be understood is data you have already lost.** The dominant pattern remains centripetal: readings stream from the edge to a distant cloud to be aggregated, modeled, and — crucially — decrypted for use. Two failure modes hide inside that convenience. The first is confidentiality over time. Encryption that protects a transmission today does not protect it against an adversary who records the ciphertext now and decrypts it later once a cryptographically relevant quantum computer exists — the harvest-now-decrypt-later exposure that motivated the standardization of post-quantum algorithms (National Institute of Standards and Technology [NIST], 2024) [demonstrated]. For infrastructure telemetry, grid topologies, and subscriber records with multi-decade sensitivity, a message intercepted in 2026 is a message potentially readable in 2036. The second failure mode is availability, and it is the one this paper's falsifier turns on: if a model of reality can only be read after the data has traveled to a place, then losing the path to that place means losing the ability to see reality at all.

**A small, concrete instance of that failure mode.** During a power outage at the Vöfab district-heating deployment in Växjö, the on-site control layer lost both grid power and its uplink at once [deployed]. The plant itself was fine — the physical reality of pipes, water, and heat persisted exactly as before — but the *readable model* of that reality went dark, because the model lived somewhere the operator could no longer reach. Nothing about the heat changed; only access to knowing it did. This is the partition test in miniature, and it earns its place precisely because it is undramatic: no war, no cyberattack, just a routine outage exposing an architecture that had quietly made reality contingent on reachability.

**The stakes scale far past one heating plant.** When the partition is national rather than local, the same architecture fails at civilizational scale. Ukraine's telecom sector stayed reachable through the grid-power loss and national-roaming events described in Chapter 1 precisely because reachability had been engineered not to depend on any single place. The counter-lessons are just as instructive: the Kyivstar wipe demonstrated that "in the cloud" is not the same as "safe," and heavy reliance on a single satellite vendor created its own chokepoint. The lesson generalizes cleanly — *no data should depend on one reachable place* — and it indicts the centripetal default directly.

**The fourth problem is governance built for paper.** Even where the technology is sound, the operating model often is not. Sidewalk Toronto is the cautionary reference: an ambitious data-rich urban project whose governance and data-stewardship questions could not be resolved and which was wound down in 2020 (Sidewalk Labs, 2020) [demonstrated]. The failure was not sensors or software; it was the absence of a credible, explainable, sovereign answer to *who controls the recording of reality and on what terms.* Governance designed as a static document — signed once, filed, and unread — cannot govern a system that records continuously for decades. Standards bodies have begun encoding the alternative: autonomous-network maturity is now framed as a graded capability ladder rather than a binary (TM Forum, n.d.) [demonstrated], which at least gives operators an honest way to say where they actually stand instead of where the brochure says they are.

**The need, crystallized.** Put the four together — artifacts instead of reality, holes patched with dead statistics, data that must leave to be understood, and governance written for paper — and the situation is not a set of independent defects to be patched. It is a single misplaced trust: trust in the readout over the reality it points at. Today holds only as long as nothing tests it. The remainder of this paper is about building the thing that survives the test — and about the fact that the decisions determining whether it does are being locked in now, not later.

## 3. Reality Under Duress: Wildfires, Hurricanes, Earthquakes

On September 27, 2024, the rain gauge on the roof of the National Centers for Environmental Information in Asheville, North Carolina, kept recording. It measured the water that was, at that moment, cutting the building off from the nation it served. Hurricane Helene had turned the French Broad River into a battering ram; the campus that houses the largest archive of the planet's climate memory sat dark, its staff evacuated, its fiber severed. The instrument still read reality correctly. Nobody could reach the reading. That is the problem of this chapter in a single image: the measurement survived and the *access to it* did not.

This is the pattern that recurs across every recent disaster, and it is the pattern the previous chapter's thesis predicts. When infrastructure meets duress, the network and the data tend to die *together* — and restoration crews then work blind, rebuilding a reality they can no longer read.

**Helene: the backhaul lesson.** At Helene's peak the U.S. Federal Communications Commission recorded that 48.7% of cell sites across the disaster area were out of service (FCC, 2024) [deployed]. The instructive detail is not the headline percentage but its cause. Comparatively few sites were physically destroyed; most went dark because the *backhaul* — the fiber and microwave links carrying traffic from the tower to the core — was severed, or because generators exhausted their fuel before roads reopened (FCC, 2024) [deployed]. A cell can be structurally intact, powered, and useless, because the one path connecting it to a distant core is under a landslide. Meanwhile, the NOAA Asheville archive — decades of irreplaceable observational record — was imperilled not by data corruption but by loss of physical reachability to the facility (NOAA, 2024) [demonstrated]. The reading was fine. The road to the reading was gone.

The forward design follows directly. A sovereign edge does not treat the backhaul link as a precondition for function. It caches the readable model of its own patch of reality locally, serves it to whoever is physically near, and reconciles upstream when a path returns — any path, not *the* path. Under this design, a tower cut off from its core still answers the emergency responder standing beneath it, and the local twin of the network's own state (which cells live, which neighbors are reachable, what capacity remains) is legible on site rather than inferred from a dashboard three states away that has itself gone dark [projection].

**Iberia: when the power axis fails cleanly.** On April 28, 2025, the Iberian Peninsula lost roughly 15 gigawatts of generation in about five seconds, and Spain and Portugal went dark in a near-total grid collapse (Red Eléctrica de España, 2025) [deployed]. This was not water or wind tearing at physical plant; it was the electrical substrate itself vanishing while towers, fiber, and data centers stood physically pristine. Mobile networks degraded as battery reserves drained and diesel ran down — a clean demonstration that the failure axis is not "is the equipment intact?" but "can this node function without the two things a disaster removes first, grid power and the far core?" The event isolates the variable. Everything was built and undamaged; it still stopped, because it was architecturally dependent on a supply that a five-second fault erased.

The degraded-mode answer is a rehearsed, not improvised, descent through capability tiers: full service, then local-cache-only service on battery, then a minimal always-reachable state layer that survives on the smallest possible power envelope. The distinction from today's practice is the word *rehearsed*. A failover path that has never been exercised is a hypothesis, not a capability — a point this paper returns to as a design discipline.

**Jamaica: the single-vendor mirage.** Hurricane Melissa's 2025 assault on Jamaica put the newest resilience story to its first real test: Starlink Direct-to-Cell, satellite-to-standard-phone service meant to keep handsets alive when terrestrial towers fall. It worked as a stopgap and it exposed a structural trap. Restoring reachability by routing an entire nation's fallback through one operator's constellation replaces a distributed dependency with a *single* one (ITU, 2025) [demonstrated]. This is the same lesson the origin case taught from the other direction: Ukraine's wartime reliance on Starlink created a single-vendor chokepoint even as it delivered genuine connectivity (Chapter 1). Direct-to-cell is a valuable *layer*. Treated as *the* layer, it recreates the very fragility it was bought to cure.

The forward design does not reject satellite failover; it refuses to make it sovereign over the local truth. The edge node keeps functioning and keeps its reality readable whether the return path is fiber, microwave, one satellite constellation, a second, or a mesh to the node next door. Failover is plural by construction, and the local model is authoritative until an upstream path — any of several — is re-established.

**The partition test, as a written requirement.** Across wildfire, hurricane, and earthquake, the three cases collapse to one question, and it is the falsifier named in this paper's introduction. The **partition test**: *does the readable model of reality survive the simultaneous loss of network and power at the node?* Not "is there a backup in the cloud" — Helene's severed backhaul and Iberia's vanished grid both sit *between* the node and any cloud. Not "is there a satellite" — Jamaica showed that outsources the answer to one vendor. The test is local and binary. If a responder standing at a dark tower, holding a phone with no bars and a dying battery, can still read the true present state of the reality that tower observes, the node passes. If that state lived only in a data center now unreachable by severed fiber, or only in a dashboard now unpowered, the node fails — and every crew downstream restores blind.

Stated as a procurement-grade requirement rather than an aspiration: **every node must hold, locally and on minimal power, a model of its own reality that remains readable and reconcilable when both its network path and its grid supply are gone, and this survival must be rehearsed, not assumed** [target]. Helene shows the network path fails first; Iberia shows the power path fails cleanest; Melissa shows that a single restoration vendor is not a substitute for local survival. The archive on the Asheville roof kept reading the flood correctly the entire time. The architecture failure was never in the instrument. It was in the decision to make the reading reachable from only one place — the decision the chapters ahead show we are still free, for a narrowing window, to make differently.

## 4. The Method: Reality Engineering with SMILE

Every argument in this paper runs on a single analytic method, and intellectual honesty requires naming it before deploying it. The method is SMILE — the **Sustainable Methodology for Interoperable Lifecycle Enablement** (Waern, 2026, DOI 10.5281/zenodo.19646240). The name is not decoration. A network built to stand for fifty years is not a technology problem that resolves at commissioning; it is a *lifecycle* problem whose hardest requirement is that each generation of equipment, each standard, each operator, and each successor system be able to read and act on what came before. That is *interoperable lifecycle enablement*: the capacity of a reality-recording to remain legible and actionable across vendors, decades, and crises. It is the correct lens precisely because the failure mode of long-lived infrastructure is never a single component — it is the silent loss of the ability to interpret one's own past.

**One inquiry logic, named once.** This paper's inquiry logic is Rackham's SPIN — Situation, Problem, Implication, Need (Rackham, 1988) — a discovery framework built to surface a need the reader has not yet articulated. In this paper, SPIN refers only to Rackham's four-stage questioning sequence; it denotes nothing spatial and no "twinning" construct. Applied here, SPIN establishes the reader's **Situation** (they operate infrastructure whose reality-recording is assumed, not engineered), exposes the **Problem** (the recording depends on a single reachable place), draws out the **Implication** (the compounding fifty-year cost of that dependency), and crystallizes the **Need** (a sovereign, explainable, quantum-safe recording of reality). It is named once here and used deliberately thereafter.

### 4.1 AEST: temporal operations at an anchor

SMILE treats any chosen point in reality — a building, a radio cell, a plant — as an *anchor* at which four temporal operations become available. **Absorb** the past: ingest institutional memory and the historical record. **Emulate** the living present: maintain a present-state twin calibrated to physical measurement. **Simulate** forward: test interventions in scenario space before committing physical resources. **Transcend**: define a desired sustainable future state and reverse-engineer the path from it back to present action — structured reverse causation from a target, not speculation (Waern, 2026).

The Rakuten Shibuya Open RAN case (Chapter 5) makes AEST concrete rather than abstract. **Absorb**: the cluster's handover history constitutes the institutional memory. **Emulate**: the live twin tracks per-neighbour-relation KPIs, exposing the problem relations performing below the fleet. **Simulate**: candidate Cell Individual Offset (CIO) adjustments are tested against the emulated topology before any parameter is pushed to a live cell. **Transcend**: the target success rate defines the goal, and the CIO path back to it is computed. The full result set is stated once in §5.1–§5.2 and cross-referenced by name here rather than repeated. The method is not "collect data and hope"; it moves from a defined future backward to the action that reaches it.

### 4.2 NUDEDA: need before data

SMILE inverts the dominant order of practice. Under **NUDEDA**, work proceeds from an identified *Need* to the *Data* requirements that serve it — never data-first. One does not instrument a network and mine for meaning; one states the outcome (raise handover success on the worst neighbour-relations), derives the insight required (which relations, by how much, weighted how), and only then specifies the data. The zero-gaslight discipline of Chapter 5 — reporting the attempt-weighted rate rather than a flattering unweighted mean — is NUDEDA applied to integrity: the need is an honest readout, so the data is chosen to serve honesty rather than flatter it.

### 4.3 Six phases ending in Perpetual Wisdom

SMILE is phase-sequential and concentric — later phases add capability without discarding earlier layers. The phases run from Reality Emulation (establish the shared referent), through concurrent engineering of a minimal viable twin, ontology and interoperability grounding, real-time connected operation, and edge-native autonomous intelligence, to **Phase 6: Perpetual Wisdom**. Phase 6 is the load-bearing phase for this paper's thesis: knowledge persists beyond project closure through *perpetual reality recording*. The recording does not end when a deployment does; it becomes the baseline from which the next deployment begins. This is why the falsifier of the whole paper — the partition test — is a Phase 6 property: a reality-recording that cannot survive the loss of network and power was never perpetual to begin with.

### 4.4 Four perspectives

Each phase is worked from four simultaneous perspectives, functioning as quality gates rather than planning silos. **From People**: stakeholder enrolment, concurrent engineering, human-machine interaction. **From Systems**: standards, ontology alignment, interoperability mechanisms. **From Planet**: spatial-temporal grounding, environmental context, circular and ESG constraints. **From AI**: model selection, explainability, edge inference, trust-boundary definition — AI treated as an actant that transforms the network it joins (Callon, 1986), not a tool subordinate to Systems.

### 4.5 The Crucible trust layer

Governing every AI output in SMILE is **Crucible**: retrieval over generation, explainability by design. When accuracy matters, the twin retrieves the measured value rather than generating a plausible one. Crucible enforces provenance at the data level (every point carries source, timestamp, confidence, validation status), source attribution at the inference level (every output traces to the graph entries that produced it), and explainability at the decision level (a domain expert can read the causal chain and override it). An output that cannot be explained in domain language has not passed the gate, regardless of statistical accuracy (Arrieta et al., 2020). For physically governed systems, this favours Large Quantitative Models — physics-based simulation with explicit uncertainty — over statistical pattern-matching, reducible to Small Quantitative Models for millisecond edge inference (Waern, 2026).

### 4.6 Five theoretical pillars

SMILE rests on five established foundations, situating it within the Gothenburg School Five Model lineage (Magoulas & Pessi, 1998): **Design Science Research** (the methodology as a constructed, evaluable artefact; Hevner et al., 2004); **Actor-Network Theory** (the twin as obligatory passage point; Callon, 1986; Latour, 2005); **boundary object theory** (one referent serving many communities without forced consensus; Star & Griesemer, 1989); **absorptive capacity** (path-dependent knowledge accumulation; Cohen & Levinthal, 1990); and **benefits management** (benefits are actively delivered, never automatic; Ward & Daniel, 2006). The through-line is a single thesis: **reality itself — physically measured, digitally rendered — is the boundary-spanning object**, the cave-wall representation through which people, systems, and AI coordinate. Not a model of reality, but reality recorded.

### 4.7 The honest circularity, and the paper's own method

Two disclosures close this chapter. First, a **circularity**: SMILE is both the author's method *and* an artefact under evaluation in this paper. The RAN case tests the network thesis; it does not independently validate SMILE, whose broader claims remain design-science propositions pending multi-site confirmation (Hevner et al., 2004). This is stated plainly rather than smoothed over.

Second, the **paper's overall method** is evidence synthesis anchored to one primary empirical case (Rakuten Shibuya Open RAN), triangulated against documented resilience events (Kyiv, Hurricane Helene, the Iberian blackout). Every claim in this paper carries an evidence tier: **[deployed]** (observed in a production system), **[demonstrated]** (shown in a controlled or pilot setting, or reported by a third party), **[projection]** (modelled forward estimate), and **[target]** (a stated design goal, not yet met). The reader is entitled to know, at every sentence, which of these they are being asked to believe — and on what basis. That labelling is the method's answer to its own circularity: the argument earns trust by marking its seams, not hiding them.

## 5. The Network That Heals Itself: Primary Evidence

A power cut proves an edge node can keep its own mind when the world goes dark. It does not prove the harder thing this paper promises: that a network can notice its own decay and correct it before a human reaches for the keyboard. Degradation is subtler than disaster. The towers stay lit, the dashboard stays green, and yet somewhere in the mesh of neighbor relations a handful of cells are quietly handing moving users to the wrong tower — dropping calls one at a time, in a pattern no aggregate KPI will surface until the churn report arrives a quarter later. This chapter closes that gap with measured numbers rather than architecture rhetoric. It is the empirical spine of the falsifiable claim.

**Reproducibility caveat, stated at first use.** The dataset analyzed here is proprietary WINNIIO/Altiostar/Rakuten data, and no third party can currently reproduce these numbers. The raw handover counters are production data [deployed]; the handover-*outcome* classification carries a synthetic-mobility watermark (see §5.1), so every outcome-derived figure below is labeled [demonstrated], never [deployed]. The single-operator, single-metro, single-KPI-family scope is the central evidence limitation of the entire paper, consolidated again in Chapter 11 but flagged here so no reader mistakes one dataset for settled science.

### 5.1 The dataset, and its honest baseline

WINNIIO analyzed a production mobility dataset from Rakuten Mobile's dense-urban Shibuya Open RAN cluster: **75 cells and 12,819,049 handover attempts**. Overall baseline handover success measured **97.87%** [demonstrated]. That number is a warning, not a reassurance. Well-run dense-urban clusters typically sit at 98.5–99.5%, so 97.87% is a *soft* baseline — high enough to look healthy on a summary tile, low enough that an attentive operator would already be asking questions. Six cells breached the problem threshold (handover failure above 5%, ping-pong above 2.7%), their local pain averaged away by the fleet-wide mean. The aggregate was hiding the very failures worth fixing.

Why the handovers failed matters as much as how often. The canonical mobility-robustness taxonomy — too-early, too-late, wrong-cell (Nguyen & Kwon, 2018) [peer-reviewed-theory] — is properly derived from radio-link-failure reports, which this dataset does not carry. From attempt counters plus ping-pong timing alone, we classify heuristically: relations with rapid return handovers score as ping-pong/too-early; relations whose failures concentrate toward a non-serving neighbor score as wrong-cell. On that heuristic, wrong-cell handovers account for roughly 72% of failures on otherwise-healthy cells — a labeled inference from counters, not an RLF-verified split [demonstrated, inference]. The direction is the point. Wrong-cell dominance is the signature that Cell Individual Offset (CIO), the per-neighbor spatial bias, is the correct lever; too-early/too-late failures would instead point to timing (time-to-trigger, hysteresis). The diagnosis chooses the treatment.

### 5.2 The intervention, and a footnote that refuses to flatter

The self-healing action was CIO tuning through Mobility Robustness Optimization: shift the offset on a mistuned neighbor relation by one to two decibels so the handover boundary moves off the fade cliff where calls were breaking. **Across 22 re-optimized neighbor relations, problem-cell handover success rose from approximately 84% to approximately 92% on the acted relations — a mean improvement of +8.0 percentage points, with a maximum of +11.1 points** [demonstrated]. Two numbers must never be conflated: the ~84% and ~92% endpoints are the attempt-weighted mean across the acted relations (84 + 8.0 = 92), while +11.1 is the single best-improving relation. This is a closed control loop acting on the operator's own live telemetry. Its direction of travel is consistent with the O-RAN near-real-time RIC literature on real-time A3-threshold and CIO tuning over the E2SM-RC interface, which reports comparable handover-failure reductions [demonstrated] (see, e.g., O-RAN ALLIANCE use-case reporting on RIC-based mobility optimization). The mechanistic literature frames CIO as one of the three self-organizing-network pillars (Onireti et al., 2016; Asghar et al., 2018) [peer-reviewed-theory]; our contribution is a *measured* instance on a nationwide commercial Open RAN, not a testbed.

One footnote earns the reader's trust for everything above it. Naive reporting would average the success rate across the 22 relations and claim a larger, cleaner lift. When we reconciled that unweighted mean-of-rates against the attempt-weighted rate, the naive average inflated success by roughly **0.7–0.8 percentage points** [demonstrated]. Small cells with few attempts but near-perfect rates drag the mean upward; weighting by the 12.8 million attempts that actually happened corrects it. We report the weighted number and name the inflation. This is the zero-gaslight discipline the whole paper argues for, stated once here as method and cross-referenced by name elsewhere: an autonomous system that optimizes against a flattering average will confidently heal the wrong thing. The attempts that occurred are the arbiter; the vanity metric is not.

### 5.3 Where this sits on the autonomy ladder

Mapped to the TM Forum Autonomous Networks framework, this loop is **Level 3 — conditional autonomy, scenario-scoped to mobility robustness** (TM Forum, 2024a). The system detects the degraded relations, decides the CIO adjustment, and acts; a human still sets policy and reviews outcomes. Per the IG1230 framework, autonomy is assessed per task across awareness, analysis, decision, and execution within a scoped domain — not as a single global grade. Human policy-and-review is the defining signature of L3. **L4 would require removing the human from the review loop and demonstrating self-optimization across varying conditions and domains without operator policy intervention.** This loop does neither, so it is L3 with an explicit, named L4 gap. Honesty about that gap is what makes the surrounding claims credible.

The economics reported at L4 belong to specific certified use cases, and are stated as such. China Mobile reports a greater-than-30% cut in backend O&M manpower and roughly 30% lower fault MTTR in a single certified network-operations-center use case; Telefónica Vivo reports up to 90% efficiency gains in route convergence in a single network-creation use case (TM Forum, 2024a, 2024b) [TM Forum-reported operator self-claim, single certified use case]. No operator has reached organization-wide L4. Rakuten's own nationwide RIC platform targets a 15–20% energy reduction via predictive rApps (Rakuten Mobile & Rakuten Symphony, 2025a) [target], with a trial reaching up to 25% (Rakuten Mobile & Rakuten Symphony, 2025b) [demonstrated]. The self-healing MRO in this chapter is the *quality* analogue of that *energy* work: the same closed loop, the same L3 rung, a different objective.

### 5.4 The twin doing all three things at once

In Grieves' digital-twin terms, the Shibuya twin performs every function on live counters. It **replicates** the handover state of 75 cells as a continuously updated model of what the radio reality *is*; it **predicts** which neighbor relations will fail from the wrong-cell signature before the failures compound; and it **navigates** the network back toward health by re-issuing CIO offsets (Grieves, 2019). This is not three tools bolted together but one boundary object — the shared, machine-actionable representation of reality that the RIC, the operations engineer, and the optimization loop all read from and write to in their own vocabularies. It is also SMILE's AEST temporal model running at a network anchor (Waern, 2026 [demonstrated]): *Absorb* the cluster's handover history, *Emulate* the living present-state of all 75 cells, *Simulate* the offset change before committing it, and *Transcend* by reverse-engineering the CIO command that carries the network from its degraded present to the target of near-invisible mobility.

This is what self-healing looks like when you strip out the keynote and keep the counters: a soft baseline honestly labeled, a failure mode correctly diagnosed, twenty-two relations lifted eight points off the fade cliff by a loop that acted on reality rather than on a flattering summary of it — and a candid ceiling on how far the autonomy actually goes. It is modest in scope and precise in claim, and that precision is exactly why an operator should want it. The next chapter prices this loop; the chapter after takes it to the one adversary it has not yet faced — the disaster that removes the power and the backhaul the loop quietly assumes.

## 6. The Operator Ledger: What Healing Is Worth

Self-healing is easy to admire and hard to fund. A network engineer who lifts a handover success rate produces something real, but "something real" does not appear on a balance sheet until it is translated into the two numbers a CFO already tracks every quarter: revenue per subscriber and cost to keep that subscriber. This chapter performs that translation as a *parameterized model with a sensitivity range* — not a single headline euro figure — so the reader can rebuild it with their own inputs rather than borrow a fabricated bottom line. The unit of the argument is money, and the discipline is that no number is asserted without a stated source or a stated assumption.

Start with the pressure the operator actually feels. Average revenue per user (ARPU) in mature markets is flat-to-declining in real terms while data volumes climb, which means margin is defended on the cost side, not won on the price side [demonstrated, industry-standard operator economics]. Against that flat ARPU sits churn, and churn is where healing earns its keep. Dropped calls and failed handovers are among the most reliably churn-correlated experience defects in operator satisfaction studies, because they are the failures a customer feels mid-sentence rather than reads on a bill [demonstrated, operator CX research]. The Shibuya Open RAN case (the +8.0 pt weighted CIO gain on the worst-served neighbor relations, §5.2) gives us a measured mechanism to price — priced on the *weighted* improvement, never the flattering mean-of-rates (§5.2).

**The retention model as a formula, not a point estimate.** Let the annual retained-value benefit of the healing behavior be:

    Benefit = N_base × f_corridor × churn × r_reduction × (SAC + M_gross)

where N_base is the subscriber base, f_corridor the fraction of subscribers routinely traversing the healed weak boundaries, churn the annual churn rate, r_reduction the *relative* churn reduction attributable to closing the experience gap, SAC the subscriber acquisition cost avoided per retained subscriber, and M_gross one year of retained gross margin (ARPU × 12 × margin). Every term is a knob the reader sets. The structure — not any single output — is the contribution.

**Illustrative range, all inputs declared.** Take a mid-market operator and vary the two most uncertain knobs across a plausible band [assumption, illustrative]: N_base = 5,000,000; ARPU = €15/month; gross margin = 60% (so M_gross ≈ €108); SAC = €120; churn = 18%; f_corridor = 1–3%; r_reduction = 10–20%. The formula then yields an annual retained-value benefit from this one healing behavior on one corridor spanning roughly **€0.25M to €1.6M**, with the midpoint near €0.6M. The width of that band — more than a 6× spread from two knobs alone — is the honest headline: the mechanism is real and the order of magnitude is defensible, but any single euro figure would be false precision. Scale the same pattern across a metro network's dozens of weak corridors and the retention line, not a new spectrum auction, becomes the funding case for autonomy.

**Cost is the second half of the ledger, and here the lever is energy.** The radio access network is the dominant energy consumer in a mobile operator, and energy is a large and rising share of network opex [demonstrated]. This is precisely why the first Level-4 autonomous-network deployments target energy: Rakuten operates closed-loop L4 RAN-energy optimization, on the same TM Forum L0–L5 axis on which Telefónica runs L4 at portfolio scale and China Mobile runs L4 in the NOC [deployed, TM Forum Autonomous Networks]. As a parameterized entry: if RAN energy is E annually and closed-loop control trims a fraction s off it, opex saving = E × s. With E = €90M/year (assume 10% of service revenue) and s = 8–15% (a band inside demonstrated traffic-aware RAN-energy results) [assumption grounded in deployed results], that is roughly **€7M–€14M/year**, recurring, with no ARPU risk, plus the labor arbitrage of L4 loops replacing manual optimization tickets. Healing shows up twice: once as retained revenue, once as avoided cost.

**The more interesting entry is the one that does not exist yet:** revenue the twin *creates* rather than protects. A perpetually-recorded, explainable network twin turns the network from a cost center into a product surface. Three lines are visible today in demonstrated or standardized form. First, verifiable-SLA slices — network slices whose guarantees are readable, attestable state, sold to enterprises that will pay a premium for a latency or availability figure they can audit rather than trust [target, standards-supported via TMF921 intent]. Second, integrated sensing and communication (ISAC), where the same radio infrastructure sells sensing-as-a-service — presence, motion, environmental sensing — a new metered product on installed assets [projection, standards-nascent]. Third, enterprise digital twins delivered as a service to venues, campuses, and municipalities that want a live model of their own connectivity fabric [projection]. None of these should be oversold: the slice and ISAC standards are ratified-but-nascent, and revenue is unproven at scale. But each is a line the twin makes *possible* and the legacy OSS makes *impossible*.

**Finally, the asymptote.** A 100% handover success rate is not a promise; it is a limit the architecture can approach but not reach. The path runs through multi-agentic twinning — neighbor cells whose twins negotiate a *rehearsed handover contract* before the subscriber crosses the boundary, so the target cell has reserved the resource and pre-validated the path rather than discovering the request at the moment of need [target]. Each increment of rehearsal buys a fraction of the residual failure rate, with diminishing returns. The operator ledger, then, is not a claim that healing pays for itself overnight. It is a disciplined statement that retained churn, trimmed energy opex, and twin-mediated new ARPU are three quantifiable pools, that the Shibuya +8.0 pt gain (§5.2) is the measured mechanism linking engineering to the first of them, and that the reader can now put their own numbers into the same formula and read off their own range.

## 7. Capturing the Skilled Act: Resilience Against the Human Shortage

A master welder retires on a Friday, and forty years of knowing — by the color of the puddle, by the pitch of the arc — of exactly when the metal is about to run, walks out the door with him. No sensor recorded it. No document holds it. On Monday, the apprentice starts over.

This is the partition test turned on people. Chapter 5 asked whether the readable model of a network survives the loss of grid and backhaul. The same question now turns on human expertise: does the skilled act survive the loss of the single human who holds it? Today, overwhelmingly, it does not. And the demographics guarantee the loss is not a trickle but a wave.

### 7.1 The shortage is structural, not cyclical

Two well-sourced figures set the scale. In United States manufacturing, an estimated 3.8 million positions must be filled by 2033, of which roughly 1.9 million could go unfilled for lack of required skills [projection, advocacy-org source] (Deloitte & The Manufacturing Institute, 2024). Healthcare faces a shortfall of approximately 10 million health workers by 2030, concentrated in low- and middle-income countries [projection] (World Health Organization, 2022). These two are enough to establish the trend; the point does not require stacking every sector forecast. Welding illustrates the concentration — a workforce whose average age sits near 55, replacing itself slowly against steady demand [projection, advocacy-org source] (American Welding Society, 2024) — and construction reports the same wall, with a majority of firms unable to fill skilled positions [demonstrated] (Associated General Contractors of America & NCCER, 2025).

The mechanism beneath these figures is tacit knowledge — the judgment learned in the hands and never written down, which is precisely why it cannot be photocopied or emailed to a successor. Tacit knowledge has been a central construct in the knowledge-management literature since Polanyi (1966) and Nonaka & Takeuchi (1995) [peer-reviewed-theory]; the practical estimates that "most" of an expert's operative knowledge is tacit vary widely by source and method and should be read as directional, not precise. A retirement is therefore not a staffing gap that a job posting closes; it is the deletion of a single-homed node. In SMILE terms, the workforce has no partition tolerance: expertise lives in one reachable person, and "no knowledge should depend on one reachable place" applies to human beings exactly as it applies to data centers.

### 7.2 The skilled act becomes a network asset

The opportunity is concrete: capture the skilled act itself — the contact-rich, judgment-laden motion — before its holder leaves, and re-teach it to the novices who follow. Embodied AI now makes this technically credible, and the enabling method is imitation learning: robots acquiring complex manual skills directly from human demonstration rather than from hand-coded rules [demonstrated]. Critically, the frontier has reached contact-rich tasks — the grinding, seating, and seam-following where force and feel dominate — which recent surveys document as now learnable from demonstration (Tsuji et al., 2026) [demonstrated]. These are exactly the tacit acts that resisted every prior codification attempt.

The transfer layer is the humanoid foundation model. NVIDIA's Isaac GR00T N1 is an open vision-language-action model trained on real-robot demonstrations, human video, and synthetic data, converting a human demonstration into a transferable robot skill and outperforming prior imitation-learning baselines (NVIDIA, 2025; Zhao et al., 2025, arXiv:2503.14734) [demonstrated]. The physical substrate to receive those skills exists at scale: the International Federation of Robotics reports an operational stock of industrial robots in the millions of units and rising year over year (International Federation of Robotics, 2025) [deployed]. The bodies are on the floor; the question is whose knowledge runs on them.

The digital twin is the knowledge-transfer layer that makes the capture durable rather than momentary. Twins standardize and scale knowledge transfer — letting operators rehearse changeovers and onboard novices without pausing production [demonstrated]. The captured act is not left as a raw motion log; it is deposited into the twin's ontology as a queryable, versioned procedure that survives the demonstrator's departure. This is the SMILE reading made precise: the skilled act is captured across the **People** and **Systems** perspectives (the four perspectives of §4.4) and persists in **Phase 6 Perpetual Wisdom** beyond the tenure of any single actor. The AEST operations apply directly: the twin **Absorbs** the retiring expert's demonstrated history, **Emulates** the current best-practice procedure as a living present-state model, and lets an apprentice **Simulate** the act safely before touching hot metal.

### 7.3 Bidirectional: humans teach robots, robots re-teach humans

The value is not one-directional automation. The capture is bidirectional: the master demonstrates, the model absorbs, and the model then re-teaches the next human. Structured robotic-assisted-surgery training programs offer published evidence that the twin-plus-robot combination functions as a staffing-resilience multiplier — expanding what a fixed human team can safely cover rather than replacing the team [demonstrated] (operating-room training study, 2025). The pattern generalizes across skill-dense sectors: in steel, AI predictive-maintenance approaches codify veteran diagnostic knowledge into models precisely as the maintenance cohort that holds that sense retires [demonstrated]; in precision manufacturing and hospitals alike, the twin becomes the medium through which one remaining expert's judgment reaches many novices at once — the knowledge-transfer role of §7.2 applied to the human act rather than to the physical asset.

### 7.4 Why the skill model must be a sovereign edge asset

Here the workforce argument rejoins the paper's spine, and the architecture decision is the whole game. A captured skill model is the distilled, irreplaceable expertise of a workforce. If that model is locked inside a vendor cloud, the organization has not solved its resilience problem; it has relocated it — trading dependence on one retiring human for dependence on one reachable vendor and one reachable network. The partition test is unforgiving: a skill model that goes dark when the backhaul drops has failed exactly as the Kyivstar wipe and the Starlink single-vendor chokepoint of Chapter 1 failed. A welding-arc model that cannot run on the shop-floor edge when the WAN is down is not resilient knowledge; it is rented knowledge.

The requirement, therefore, is that captured skill models be edge-deployable and sovereign — an organizational asset, not a leased capability. SMILE's edge-native commitment supports this directly: a Large Quantitative Model of a physical act can be reduced to a Small Quantitative Model that runs on local hardware at millisecond latency (Waern, 2026), so the skilled act remains executable and re-teachable under partition. This is the standard the Crucible trust layer (§4.5) must enforce on any acquired skill model: retrieval over generation (the apprentice is shown the master's recorded, provenance-signed act, not a plausible hallucination of it) and explainability by design (the model can show *why* the puddle is about to run, in the master's own causal terms, not merely mimic the motion).

The future this chapter names is thus specific and buildable: the retiring master's four decades do not walk out on Friday. They are absorbed into a sovereign twin, they run at the edge when the network does not, and on Monday they teach the apprentice — reachable under partition, owned by the people whose hands first held them.

## 8. The Reality-Capture Stack: Splats, World Models, and Sovereign Economies

Kyiv proved the negative: when the network partitions and the grid fails, a model that lives in one reachable place dies with that place (Chapter 1). This chapter builds the positive. If reality is the primary, perpetually-recorded, boundary-spanning construct, then the question for the next fifty years is procedural — *how* do we capture reality faithfully, fold it into models that predict, address it so any agent can reach it, and let the people it describes own it? Four technology layers now answer that question, each maturing at a different rate, each mappable onto the same replicate–predict–navigate arc Grieves gave the digital twin (Chapter 5). This chapter separates the deployed from the aspirational, layer by layer, and marks every claim.

### 8.1 CAPTURE — space made photoreal and addressable

The first layer turns physical space into a dense, queryable record. Kerbl et al. (2023) introduced 3D Gaussian splatting, reconstructing real scenes from ordinary photographs into a representation that renders photorealistically in real time [demonstrated, SIGGRAPH]. The advance matters less for its frame rate than for what it captures: not a designer's idealized CAD model of what a building *should* be, but a measured record of what a place actually *is* — down to the rust, the sag, the improvised repair. This is the cave wall rendered at survey fidelity (Chapter 3).

The primitive has already left the lab. Bentley Systems integrated splatting into its iTwin reality-modeling pipeline for infrastructure engineering [deployed] (Bentley Systems, 2024). Commercial capture workflows now compress a site scan to a short 360-degree walkthrough that exports a labeled, simulation-ready twin into robotics simulators [deployed/demonstrated] (Insta360 & Splatica, 2025). Construction research has coupled splatting to BIM under ISO 19650 coordination standards [peer-reviewed], and robotics groups build splatting twins precise enough to close the sim-to-real loop for manipulation [demonstrated-preprint]. For a telecom operator, the implication is concrete: the cell site, the cabinet, the antenna tilt are no longer a spreadsheet row and a decade-old photo but a re-capturable spatial record — the substrate a self-healing network needs to reason about its own physical body.

### 8.2 MODEL — reality folded into prediction

Capture yields a faithful *present*. The second layer aims to learn the *dynamics*. A line of recent preprints proposes "World Action Models" (WAMs) — reportedly a term introduced by NVIDIA around early 2026, a provenance we cannot independently confirm and therefore flag [early-preprint]. The stated goal is a joint model over future states and the actions that produce them, replacing the classic perception→planning→control pipeline with an end-to-end learned model [projection, early-preprint]. One such preprint describes a ~14-billion-parameter model reaching, by the preprint's own self-reported figure, roughly 62% task progress and transferring to an unseen robot from about thirty minutes of teleoperation [early-preprint, unverified — figures are the preprint's self-report, not independently reproduced]. A companion survey proposes a taxonomy of cascaded versus joint architectures trained on internet-scale egocentric video [survey-preprint]. These claims rest on single, non-peer-reviewed, recent preprints and should be read as directional research signals, not results. By contrast, GR00T N1 (arXiv:2503.14734, §7.2) is a verifiable reference point.

The honest constraint is latency, and it is where the partition test bites. Photorealistic future-imagination is compute-heavy, and compute cost is decisive at the edge. The research direction of interest is distillation — compressing such models toward per-inference latencies compatible with edge power-and-compute budgets [projection, early-preprint]. Whether that trajectory delivers is unproven. The architectural point stands regardless: a predictive world model that only runs in one hyperscale region fails the same test the pre-war Ukrainian cloud stack failed.

### 8.3 ADDRESS/INTEROPERATE — the network layer for reality

Captured, modeled reality is inert unless agents from different vendors, domains, and jurisdictions can reach and read it without a private integration for every pair. This is where standards, not products, carry the weight. In 2025 the IEEE ratified P2874, the Spatial Web standard, defining HSML (a shared vocabulary for entities, activities, and relationships) and HSTP (a protocol for permissions and policy across agents, IoT, robotics, and twins) (Spatial Web Foundation, 2025) [ratified standard]. The European Commission's Web 4.0 initiative names this transition at policy level and commits, rhetorically, to open standards over monopoly [roadmap/policy] (European Commission, 2023).

The honesty flag is load-bearing: **ratification is not adoption.** IEEE 2874-2025 is real and recent, but its deployed footprint is nascent — there is no running "spatial web" to point at, only a specification and early implementers. HSML/HSTP are best read as the external-addressing complement to the Minimal Interoperability Mechanisms treated in Chapter 10: the MIMs govern the seams a city signs; HSML gives external agents a shared vocabulary to name the reality a twin represents. Both are standards to build toward, not capabilities to claim as shipped.

### 8.4 OWN/TRANSACT — sovereignty as economics

The final layer decides who owns the perpetual record and who gets paid when a machine reads it. The durable substrate is the W3C stack: Decentralized Identifiers (DIDs), ratified as a Recommendation in 2022, paired with Verifiable Credentials, enable self-sovereign, cryptographically verifiable identity for people, organizations, devices, and agents (Sporny et al., 2022) [W3C standard]. The "Web5" marketing label sometimes placed over this stack is contested and directional rather than deployed, and this paper ignores it; the substance is DIDs and VCs. Machine-to-machine value-exchange rails — stablecoin micropayments and blockchain foundations for agent economies — are emerging independently and remain [roadmap/early].

Strip the branding and the architecture is coherent with everything preceding it. A DID lets a sovereign individual, or a sovereign community node, hold the identity of a captured place and gate who may query its model. A Verifiable Credential lets a self-healing network prove an intent came from an authorized operator without a central authority to phone. Micropayment rails let one agent pay another for a slice of reality data at machine speed. This is "no data should depend on one reachable place" (Chapter 1) expressed as economics rather than topology: ownership that survives the loss of any single custodian.

### 8.5 The stack as one argument

The four layers compose into a single, tier-honest claim. CAPTURE (splatting) is **deployed**. MODEL (WAMs) is **early-preprint** and unproven at the edge. ADDRESS (IEEE 2874, W3C DIDs) is **ratified but nascent** — real standards, early adoption. TRANSACT (agent economies) is **emerging**. No layer is oversold, and the gaps are named. What unites them is direction: each converts a piece of Kyiv's negative proof into positive infrastructure — reality made photoreal, then predictive, then addressable, then ownable. The aim this chapter builds toward is not any one product but the whole assembled *sovereignly*: captured close to the edge, modeled within the edge power budget, addressed through open standards, and owned by the people and communities the reality describes. The architecture decisions that lock this in are being made now (Chapter 1). Chapter 9 addresses one of them — the cryptographic seed — and Chapter 11 turns the rest into Monday-morning actions.

## 9. The Seed of Quantum: Protect Now, Optimize Where It Pays

A seed is not a harvest. It is a small, disciplined bet on a future you can already name — planted in the one season when planting is cheap and waiting is expensive. Quantum computing, in a paper about infrastructure that must record reality for fifty years, is exactly that: not the crop, the seed. And the honest gardener knows two things at once — where the seed already belongs in the ground today, and where planting it now would be theatre. Quantum enters the architecture in two real roles, and this chapter draws the line between them plainly.

**QR/PQC as archival ethics.** The first role is not speed. It is survival. If reality is the primary, perpetually-recorded construct (Chapter 1), then the cryptography that protects that recording must outlive the recording itself. You cannot perpetually record reality on cryptography with a ten-year life. RSA-2048 and the elliptic-curve suites securing today's telemetry, intent contracts, and twin state have a shelf life measured against an adversary who does not yet exist but is already collecting. Harvest-now, decrypt-later is a documented collection strategy, and it turns every long-lived archive into a deferred breach [demonstrated]. A NOAA climate record, a RAN configuration history, a sovereign health twin: each is a fifty-year secret encrypted with a ten-year lock.

The response is standardized and shipping. NIST ratified the first post-quantum standards in August 2024 — FIPS 203 (ML-KEM, key encapsulation), FIPS 204 (ML-DSA, digital signatures), and FIPS 205 (SLH-DSA, stateless hash-based signatures) (NIST, 2024) [deployed]. The architectural obligation is not "adopt PQC someday." It is *crypto-agility now*: the ability to rotate algorithms without re-platforming, so that the archive layer signs and encrypts with ML-DSA and ML-KEM at the moment of recording — PQC-at-archive, not PQC-at-retrofit. Retrofitting encryption onto a fifty-year archive after the fact is the same category error as bolting resilience onto a network after the partition (Chapter 4): the cost compounds precisely because you waited. For infrastructure recording reality perpetually, planting season is the day the first byte is written.

**QI optimization, benchmark-gated, and honest about today.** The second role is optimization — and here the discipline is sharper, because the hype is louder. Quantum-inspired (QI) and quantum-annealing methods can express a specific class of problems — Quadratic Unconstrained Binary Optimization (QUBO) — that maps naturally onto certain network and scheduling tasks. The self-healing RAN work at the empirical core of this paper is a candidate: CIO tuning across a dense neighbor-relation graph, where each handover boundary is a coupled binary decision, is a combinatorial surface that QUBO can represent [demonstrated]. Portfolio scheduling across a multi-vendor estate is another. These are real problem shapes, not marketing.

But representation is not victory, and this is the zero-gaslight point of the chapter: **today, classical MRO wins, and we say so.** The 22 problem neighbor-relations lifted by the +8.0 pt weighted gain in the Shibuya deployment (§5.2) were tuned by classical optimization. No quantum processor touched that result. A quantum-inspired solver that cannot beat a well-tuned classical solver on wall-clock, cost, and reproducibility has earned nothing but a footnote. The gate is empirical, not aspirational: quantum optimization enters production the day it wins a disclosed benchmark against the classical incumbent on the *same* problem instance — and not one day sooner.

**The one artifact that earns quantum its billing.** So what do we actually build now that makes the future reachable without pretending it has arrived? One concrete deliverable: **a solver-neutral problem-interchange schema paired with mandatory benchmark disclosure.** The schema expresses the optimization problem — the CIO graph, the QUBO formulation, the constraints, the objective — independently of the engine that solves it, so that a classical heuristic, a quantum-inspired annealer, and a future gate-model processor all consume the *same* problem statement and return comparable results. The benchmark disclosure is the falsifier attached to every claim: solver identity, wall-clock, cost, solution quality, and the classical baseline it must beat, published alongside the result. This is Crucible discipline (§4.5) applied to optimization — evidence over assertion. It costs little today and makes every future quantum claim auditable.

**The spine: QA → QI → QR → HQC → QN.** The quantum progression gives the sequence, and this chapter is honest about where each stage sits. **QA** (quantum-assisted) and **QI** (quantum-inspired optimization) are usable now, in the narrow, benchmark-gated cases above [demonstrated]. **QR** (quantum-resistant cryptography) is not future at all — FIPS 203/204/205 make it the present obligation of the archive layer [deployed]. **HQC** (hybrid quantum-classical compute) and **QN** (the quantum network) remain projections — real research trajectories, not deployable infrastructure [projection]. The spine is not a roadmap of inevitabilities. It is a ledger with two entries in ink (QR today, QI where it wins) and three in pencil. Protect now. Optimize where it pays. Say plainly where it does not.

## 10. Interoperability Is the Constitution: MIMs and the Boundary Object

A constitution is not a manual; it is a short list of clauses binding enough that everything else must conform to them. Smart-city interoperability has such a document, and it is deliberately sparse. The Open & Agile Smart Cities profile fixes seven load-bearing clauses — MIM1 Context, MIM2 Data Models, MIM3 Contracts, MIM4 Trust and Personal Data, MIM5 Transparency, MIM6 Security, MIM7 Places — as the Minimal Interoperability Mechanisms every conformant system must honor (Open & Agile Smart Cities & Living-in.EU Technical Sub-Group, 2024) [deployed]. "Minimal" is the whole wager: specify the fewest seams at which two systems must agree, and let everything behind each seam stay sovereign. This chapter reads the clauses at mechanism level — what precisely crosses each seam, and what breaks when it does not — using a single running case rather than an exhaustive catalogue. Smart-building interoperability appears only briefly, as the simplest instance; the disaster case carries the argument.

### 10.1 The boundary box: MIM1 context and MIM2 grammar

MIM1 does one thing: it names *whose* reality is under discussion, at what spatial extent, over what temporal window. Its ontology is Actor-Network Theory's — sensors, occupants, and institutions enter the record as peer actors, not as a hierarchy of instrument beneath subject (Waern, 2026) [demonstrated]. The concrete artifact is a boundary box with three declared fields: the anchor entity, the spatial extent (a MIM7 Places geospatial referent), and the temporal window. A context broker that cannot populate those three fields has not defined its box, and every downstream clause inherits the ambiguity.

The smart-building case shows the box populated, briefly. A building twin exposes each room as a MIM1 context entity — anchor: the room; extent: its cadastral coordinate; window: live present plus a perpetual trace. MIM2 supplies the shared grammar: the room's heat-demand attribute is expressed in an NGSI-LD data model whose semantics a municipal district-heating twin already parses, so the two twins interoperate without sharing a codebase — the demonstrated federated pattern of NGSI-LD brokers under a Gaia-X trust frame (Cirillo et al., 2024) [demonstrated]. Under the edge-native design the boundary box persists locally through a partition, and the seam becomes the reattachment point on reconnection — the property no centralised design possesses. That is as far as the building case needs to go.

### 10.2 Across communities that share no ontology: MIM3, MIM4, MIM5

The harder interoperation crosses communities with no common vocabulary at all — and this is where MIM3 Contracts, MIM4 Trust, and MIM5 Transparency carry the load. Take the load-bearing example: a network operator's twin and an emergency-services twin during a disaster. When a hurricane degrades the network, the operator must hand a slice of its live reality to responders who share none of its RAN ontology. MIM3 encodes the *contract* — which slice, for which purpose, under which usage restriction. MIM4 encodes *trust* — a verifiable credential proving the responder is who they claim before any slice is released. MIM5 encodes *transparency* — which automated action the operator's system took (a cell-outage-compensation retune, a CIO adjustment), rendered explainable to a non-RAN reader who must act on it under lives-at-stake pressure.

These clauses are not hypothetical. During the Helene backhaul severance the U.S. FCC activated the Mandatory Disaster Response Initiative, compelling cross-network roaming while sites sat offline (Congressional Research Service, 2024; FCC, 2024) [demonstrated]. Mandatory roaming *is* a MIM3-plus-MIM4 act — an inter-operator data-and-service contract, with its identity and trust exchange, imposed by regulation precisely because voluntary interoperation failed under load. When terrestrial mechanisms collapsed during Hurricane Melissa, Liberty Caribbean fell back to a rehearsed partition mitigation, activating Starlink Direct-to-Cell so standard LTE phones regained a link with no new equipment (Liberty Latin America, 2025) [deployed] — a pre-negotiated trust-and-contract path that keeps an operator twin exposing reality to responders after its own towers go silent.

Across both interoperations, one object spans the seam: not a shared model, but reality itself, digitally rendered — the boundary-spanning object precisely *because* the operator and the emergency service share no ontology yet share the same physical scene (Waern, 2026) [demonstrated]. Two mechanisms carry that seam and must not be conflated. The intent interface (TMF921) expresses the declarative *what* — an autonomous domain's intent to restore or compensate service — while the inter-operator sharing itself is a business agreement (MIM3) plus identity and trust (MIM4).

### 10.3 Governing a perpetual record

A perpetual reality record turns three clauses into governance of constitutional grade. *Who may query which slice, at which zoom, over which window* is a MIM4 question the boundary box must answer per query, because a decade-deep occupancy trace read at room resolution is a surveillance act regardless of intent. *Explainability as a right* is MIM5 made non-negotiable: no automated decision drawn from the record ships without provenance a domestic stakeholder can read — the retrieval-over-generation discipline (§4.5) the whole architecture depends on. And archival ethics is the clause ordinary security misses: a record built to outlive its assets must adopt the ratified NIST post-quantum primitives — FIPS 203, 204, 205 (NIST, 2024) [deployed] — at the archive layer first, inside an OAIS-style preservation model designed to keep digital objects legible across generations of technology and designated communities (Consultative Committee for Space Data Systems, 2012) [deployed], so reality stays readable irrespective of the vendor or format that recorded it — FAIR stated for infrastructure (Wilkinson et al., 2016) [peer-reviewed-theory].

### 10.4 Where the clauses land

Three governance regimes describe the same seam under different names. Mapping them keeps the constitution from fragmenting into rival standards:

| MIM (OASC) | TM Forum ODA function | ITU-T Y.4489 federation role |
|---|---|---|
| MIM1 Context / MIM7 Places | Party & context management; Intent (TMF921) as machine-actionable context | Twin registry + shared spatial reference |
| MIM2 Data Models | Information framework (SID) | Common data model for federated twins |
| MIM3 Contracts | Business agreement / product order | Inter-twin service and access agreement |
| MIM4 Trust & Personal Data | Identity & access management | Credential and authorisation exchange |
| MIM5 Transparency | AI governance & decision-logging | Explainable cross-twin decision record |

Two corrections the table encodes: TMF921 (Intent Management) belongs against MIM1/MIM3 as machine-actionable context and contract, not against MIM5; and MIM5 Transparency maps to AI-governance and decision-logging, not to an intent API. ITU-T Y.4489 standardises exactly this federation of independent twins through registries and communication interfaces (ITU-T, 2023) [roadmap]. That three independently authored frameworks describe the same minimal seam is *consistent with* the claim that the seam — not any single stack — is what a city actually signs when it commits to interoperability. It is not, by itself, evidence that the architecture works; convergence among standards bodies is common. Reality is the object all three are trying to keep shared; the MIMs are the clauses that make sharing it survivable.

## 11. Action: What to Sign on Monday

The quietest procurement decisions echo longest: few remember who chose the archive format, yet everyone inherits it. The architecture of reality-recording is being set in contracts signed this quarter, and those contracts will outlast the vendors, the standards bodies, and probably the careers of the people signing them. So this chapter closes on what forks the future, then hands the reader what to sign.

### The five decisions that fork the future

Each decision carries its evidence tier and its 50-year compounding cost — the price not of the wrong choice today but of the wrong choice multiplied across five decades of infrastructure that cannot easily be re-poured.

**1. Edge-native vs. cloud-first.** Kyiv restored national roaming within a week and withstood roughly 65% grid-power loss (Chapter 1) because reachability did not depend on one place; the December 2023 Sandworm wipe of ~24M subscribers [demonstrated] proved the inverse — the cloud is not a sanctuary. Hurricane Helene took 48.7% of area cell sites offline with a multi-week restoration [deployed] (FCC, 2024). *Compounding cost:* a cloud-first backbone chosen now quietly assumes uninterrupted transport for fifty years — an assumption every one of these events falsifies.

**2. Need-first vs. data-first (NUDEDA).** The Shibuya Open RAN case tuned CIO from an identified operational need, delivering the +8.0 pt weighted gain on 22 problem relations (§5.2). Data-first programs collect first and ask why later. *Compounding cost:* fifty years of accumulated telemetry that answers no question anyone asked, at storage and carbon cost that never amortizes.

**3. Minimal-interop vs. lock-in.** TM Forum's Open Digital Architecture and Open APIs, with intent expressed through TMF921, let operators reach L4 autonomy in scoped domains without a single-vendor stack [deployed] (TM Forum, 2023–2024). *Compounding cost:* proprietary bindings signed today become the migration bill of 2050 — paid in full, with interest.

**4. PQC-now vs. later.** NIST ratified ML-KEM, ML-DSA, and SLH-DSA in 2024 (FIPS 203/204/205) [deployed]. Data archived under classical cryptography today is harvest-now-decrypt-later inventory. *Compounding cost:* a 50-year archive encrypted with RSA is already compromised on the day the cryptographically relevant quantum computer arrives — retroactively, for everything ever written.

**5. Perpetual-wisdom vs. project-amnesia.** NOAA's Asheville climate archive sat imperilled when Helene flooded the region [demonstrated] — institutional memory is one disaster from deletion. SMILE's Phase 6 Perpetual Wisdom treats the record as outliving the project. *Compounding cost:* every project that closes without perpetual recording restarts the next one from zero, forever.

**Unit of contribution (restated once):** reality is the primary, perpetually-recorded, boundary-spanning construct, and the decisions that make its recording sovereign, explainable, and quantum-safe are being locked in now. **Falsifier:** the partition test — does the readable model of reality survive simultaneous loss of network *and* power? The Växjö heating outage offered a small live rehearsal of exactly this question; the Kyiv record answers it at national scale.

**Limitations (consolidated).** The empirical spine is one operator, one metro, one KPI family, and its outcome figures carry a synthetic-mobility watermark (§5.1); no third party can reproduce them, and every outcome-derived figure is [demonstrated], not [deployed]. The +8.0 pt CIO gain is a single-run demonstrated result, not a multi-season deployment, and the ~0.7–0.8 pt mean-of-rates-vs-attempt-weighted inflation is disclosed (§5.2) so no reader mistakes the friendlier average for ground truth. TM Forum autonomy levels and PQC standards are ratified but nascent in field deployment; the WAM/world-model claims of Chapter 8 rest on single unverified preprints [early-preprint]; DTC quantum stages (QA→QI→QR→HQC→QN) remain benchmark-gated projections [projection]. The operator economics of Chapter 6 are a parameterized model with a wide sensitivity band, not a forecast. None of this is settled science; all of it is signable engineering.
