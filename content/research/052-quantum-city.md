## Abstract

Urban infrastructure commissioned in 2026 will operate into the 2070s, yet the cryptography protecting it and the computational methods orchestrating it are refreshed on decade-scale cycles. This whitepaper examines where quantum computing genuinely intersects smart cities, self-healing telecommunications networks, and interoperable digital twins, separating deployed reality from demonstration, roadmap, and speculation across a 110-source evidence base. We trace the ledger from the 2021 6G vision literature to the 2026 state of standardization (ITU-R IMT-2030, TM Forum Autonomous Networks Levels, AI-RAN, network digital twins); map quantum capability onto TM Forum's L0-L5 autonomy framework and the replicate-predict-navigate loop of front-running simulation; and argue that post-quantum cryptography is the only quantum workstream with a hard deadline today, while quantum-inspired optimization is the only quantum computation deployed in production city and manufacturing contexts. We propose an edge-native, sovereignty-preserving reference architecture — problems travel to solvers, data never leaves the trust boundary — aligned with Minimal Interoperability Mechanisms and Digital Twin Consortium composability frameworks, and operationalized through the SMILE methodology (Waern, 2025): querying a visual representation of reality and receiving answers from reality itself or from continuously fused sensor, human, contextual, and simulated data, traversing past, present, and future across scales. A research agenda follows: a neutral problem-interchange schema for optimization workloads, PQC profiles for twin protocols, benchmark disclosure standards, and action-labeled datasets from instrumented infrastructure. The future is already here, unevenly distributed; the architecture decisions that determine its distribution are being made now, and they echo.

## 1. Introduction: A Tale of Three Cities

William Gibson's observation that the future is already here, merely unevenly distributed, is usually deployed as a rhetorical flourish. In urban infrastructure it is a literal, measurable condition. In 2026, a network operator can walk through three cities in a single week: one whose operational technology was architected in the 1990s, one whose digital twin and network fabric are being specified today, and — in blueprint form — one whose systems will still be running in 2046. These three cities coexist, sometimes within the same municipal boundary. This whitepaper argues that the decisive variable separating them is not compute, sensors, or algorithms, but architecture: the interoperability, autonomy, and cryptographic decisions made in 2026 will echo through urban infrastructure for decades, because the assets they govern — buildings, water networks, grids, transport corridors — have service lives measured in half-centuries while the technologies protecting and orchestrating them are refreshed on cycles an order of magnitude shorter.

### The city of the past

The first city is the one most operators actually manage. Its building automation, water, and energy systems run as siloed supervisory control stacks, each with proprietary data models, each exporting telemetry to a vendor cloud where insight is generated far from the point of action. Systematic reviews of urban digital twin deployments consistently identify heterogeneous data integration and cross-system interoperability — not modeling technology — as the dominant blockers to operational city-scale twins (Mazzetto, 2024; Lei et al., 2023). The problem is socio-technical as much as technical: twins disconnected from governance processes do not deliver value regardless of fidelity (Nochta et al., 2021). This city also carries a quieter liability. Its confidentiality rests on public-key cryptography whose effective lifetime is bounded by the arrival of a cryptographically relevant quantum computer. The most widely cited expert elicitation on this question — a survey of a small, self-selected pool of quantum-computing experts reporting subjective probability judgments, not a forecast — assigns a roughly one-in-three or higher aggregate probability to a cryptographically relevant quantum computer within the next decade, and a majority probability within fifteen years (Mosca & Piani, 2024). Because adversaries can harvest ciphertext now and decrypt it later, data generated today with confidentiality requirements of ten years or more is already inside the risk window (Csenkey & Bindel, 2025; CISA, NSA, & NIST, 2023). A city whose operational data must remain protected across a fifty-year asset life, secured by cryptography with perhaps a ten-year residual life, has an architectural debt that compounds silently.

### The city being built now

The second city is under construction, and its defining feature is that the future has arrived in patches — small, bounded, and sometimes unsustained. Quantum-annealing route optimization ran in the loop of a live public bus fleet in Lisbon in a one-week, nine-bus pilot in 2019 that has not been repeated since (Yarkoni et al., 2020; Volkswagen Group, 2019); quantum-inspired digital annealing schedules production sequences in an operating Toyota plant (Fujitsu Limited & Toyota Systems Corporation, 2022); a pilot study in Tokyo's Marunouchi district combined demand prediction with annealing and projected a reduction in waste-collection truck distance from roughly 2,300 km to about 1,000 km, though the projection never became a standing municipal service (D-Wave Systems, Groovenauts, & Mitsubishi Estate, 2020). On the network side, TM Forum's L0–L5 autonomous network levels (TM Forum, IG1218, 2021; TM Forum, IG1252, 2021) have moved from maturity narrative to auditable instrument through the levels-evaluation methodology and the associated certification program (TM Forum, IG1252, 2021, and later versions), with China Mobile operating a Level 4 network operation center (TM Forum Inform, 2025), Rakuten Mobile reporting the first Level 4 validation for a single use case — RAN energy efficiency — in a live commercial Open RAN network (Rakuten Mobile, 2026), and Telefónica reporting twelve Level 4 use cases under quarterly autonomy audits at the close of 2025 (Telefónica, 2026); both are operator-reported claims pending confirmation against TM Forum's own certification records. On the cryptographic side, NIST finalized the first three post-quantum standards in August 2024 (NIST, 2024), CNSA 2.0 sets hard exclusivity dates of 2033 for browsers, servers, and cloud services (NSA, 2022/2025), and the GSMA has published phased migration guidance for telecom operators (GSMA Post Quantum Telco Network Task Force, 2024). On the interoperability side, MIMs Plus gives more than 150 sub-national governments a minimal common mechanism set for city-to-city interoperability without mandating identical implementations (OASC & Living-in.EU Technical Sub-Group, 2024), while ISO/IEC 30173 and ITU-T Y.4489 supply the terminology and federation architecture for composable, cross-domain twins (ISO/IEC, 2023; ITU, 2023).

The uneven distribution cuts both ways, and honesty about it is a design input. Critical assessments show that classical decomposition, metaheuristics, and reinforcement learning currently outperform quantum methods for system-level urban transport optimization (Xu et al., 2026), and that useful quantum advantage in combinatorial optimization likely requires fault tolerance most near-term hardware lacks (Abbas et al., 2024). The second city is therefore not "the quantum city" in any literal sense; it is the city whose blueprints are quantum-aware — PQC on the drawings, solver interfaces abstracted so that quantum-inspired engines already in production deployment can be swapped for quantum-native ones if and when advantage materializes.

### The city of 2046

The third city is a projection, and this paper labels it as such. Its contours, however, are extrapolations of standards work already underway rather than speculation. Grieves's front-running simulation framework — replicate, predict, navigate (Grieves, 2023) — describes a city twin that runs continuously ahead of physical reality, screening failure scenarios before they occur. Network digital twins are moving from research topic to standards-track architecture (Zhou et al., 2025; ITU, 2022), and conceptual frameworks for integrating quantum modules into classical digital twin architectures for grid operation have been proposed on paper only — no quantum module has yet been integrated into any operating grid twin (Lemo et al., 2025). The 2046 city plausibly pairs a federated, front-running city twin with hybrid quantum-classical solvers for the problem classes where they earn their place, operated by networks at or approaching Level 5 autonomy under intent-based control (TM Forum, IG1230, 2022; TM Forum, IG1253, 2022). Whether Level 5 is reached by 2046, and whether quantum-native solvers displace their quantum-inspired ancestors, are open questions this paper treats as such.

### Querying reality: the SMILE framing

Across all three cities, this paper adopts a single operational lens: the SMILE methodology, a methodology previously published by the author (Waern, 2025, DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.17464804), applied here to the quantum-city context. Under this framing, the practitioner asks questions of a visual representation of reality and receives answers either from reality itself — live actuation and measurement at the edge — or from continuously fused sensor, people, context, and simulation data, traversing past, present, and future states and zooming across scales of time and space. The framing is useful here because it makes interoperability a first-order requirement rather than an afterthought: a query that must traverse building, network, grid, and mobility twins fails at the first ontology boundary that was never architected to be crossed. Edge-native processing is likewise structural rather than stylistic, since latency-critical urban applications require computation at the point of action (Khan et al., 2020), and data sovereignty is easier to guarantee when raw data never leaves the premises.

The remainder of this whitepaper examines, in turn, the self-healing network substrate the quantum city requires, the interoperability mechanisms that let its twins compose, the realistic state of quantum and quantum-inspired optimization for urban workloads, and the post-quantum cryptographic migration that must begin now. The through-line is constant: designing these properties into the second city's blueprints in 2026 is far cheaper than retrofitting them later. Retrofit remains possible — Section 5's cryptographic migration agenda is itself a retrofit program — but each deferred interface decision makes the eventual integration substantially more expensive, one procurement at a time.

## 2. State of Reality 2026: What Is Actually Deployed

Any credible architecture for the quantum city must begin with an audit of what actually runs in production today, as distinct from what has been demonstrated in controlled conditions, published as theory, or promised on roadmaps. This section applies that discipline across four layers: city-scale digital twins, autonomous network operations, AI-native radio access, and quantum technologies. Throughout, each claim carries two labels: its evidentiary status — deployed, demonstrated, or roadmap — and its source quality — peer-reviewed, independently audited, or self-reported by the vendor or operator. Conflating these categories is precisely how decade-scale architecture mistakes get made, and marketing communications must not carry the same weight as peer-reviewed benchmarks.

### 2.1 Operational City Twins: Real, Modest, and Unevenly Instrumented

Several city digital twins are genuinely operational. Zurich runs a production city twin — terrain, block, and roof models derived from LiDAR and cadastral survey — used routinely for urban-climate and heat-island analysis, high-rise planning, and architectural competitions, with the underlying 3D geodata published as open government data (Schrötter & Hürzeler, 2020). Helsinki has operated semantic CityGML models since the Kalasatama district twin, released as open data and applied to energy-efficiency and solar-potential analysis, though the program's own case literature identifies organizational and data-governance barriers as the constraint on moving from static models to dynamic twins (Ruohomäki et al., 2018; Hämäläinen, 2021). Boston's citywide 3D model has been in sustained production use since 2018 for shadow analysis, zoning capacity modeling, and sea-level-rise overlays (Boston Planning & Development Agency & Esri, 2023). Dubai launched a city-scale platform in July 2026 integrating more than 195,000 building models and 280,000 infrastructure assets for scenario modeling — a municipality-reported figure — though outcome metrics remain unpublished (Dubai Municipality, 2026).

Measured, outcome-level results are rarer than deployments. Barcelona's smart-city program is one of the few published city-scale ROI cases, with the city self-reporting approximately $58 million saved in water management and roughly $50 million per year in added parking revenue (Adler, 2016). Two caveats apply: the figures are municipal self-reports aggregated in a secondary case study from the mid-2010s, never independently audited, and they attach to the smart-city program broadly — Sentilo is a sensor platform, not a digital twin, so the figures should not be read as digital-twin ROI evidence. Virtual Singapore, by contrast, documents four capability classes — virtual experimentation, test-bedding, planning support, and R&D — but published outcome KPIs such as congestion or energy reductions remain unavailable (Singapore Land Authority / National Research Foundation, n.d.). Herrenberg's twin demonstrated genuine citizen-engagement value in real participation events for the town's mobility plan, but again without quantified ROI (Dembski et al., 2020). The systematic evidence is consistent: interoperability, data governance, and social and legal issues — not modeling technology — are the dominant blockers to operational city twins (Lei et al., 2023; Mazzetto, 2024), and twins deliver value only insofar as they connect to governance processes (Nochta et al., 2021). Standards have matured faster than deployments: ISO/IEC 30173 provides horizontal terminology (ISO/IEC, 2023), ITU-T Y.4600 and Y.4489 specify city-twin capabilities and twin federation respectively (ITU, 2022; ITU, 2023), CityGML 3.0 supplies the semantic substrate (OGC, 2021; Kutzner et al., 2020), and MIMs Plus gives 150-plus sub-national governments a minimal interoperability contract (OASC, 2024). The standards exist; the federated, cross-domain twin fabric they describe largely does not yet.

### 2.2 Autonomous Networks: Certified L4 Use Cases, Not L5 Networks

TM Forum's Autonomous Networks business requirements and framework (TM Forum, IG1218, 2021), the closed-loop reference architecture built on autonomous domains (TM Forum, IG1230, 2022), and the intent formalism and its API (TM Forum, IG1253, 2022; TM Forum, TMF921, 2024) have moved from framework to measurement instrument: the Autonomous Network Levels Evaluation Methodology, first published in 2021 and revised since, defines the L0–L5 grading criteria per operational task and makes autonomy level an auditable, certifiable claim (TM Forum, IG1252, 2021, and later versions). Huawei reports that a first operator cohort received formal certificates at a TM Forum event in November 2024 (Huawei, 2024) — a vendor-reported milestone that should be confirmed against TM Forum's own certification announcements.

The certified reality is more precise than the marketing around it. China Mobile has documented a Level 4 network operation center using intelligent agents — among the first L4 operating environments at national scale (TM Forum Inform, 2025). Rakuten Mobile reported the first TM Forum L4 validation in a live commercial Open RAN network, for a single use case — RAN energy-efficiency optimization, with operator-reported savings of roughly 20% (Rakuten Mobile, 2026). Telefónica reported closing 2025 with twelve L4 use cases across Spain, Brazil, and Germany, with quarterly autonomy audits per domain (Telefónica, 2026). Both claims rest on operator press materials; TM Forum distinguishes self-assessed levels, TM Forum-assessed scenario certifications, and whole-network levels, and readers should apply that distinction here. The load-bearing point remains: these are L4 *use cases* within bounded domains, not L4 *networks*, and to the authors' knowledge no certified L5 network claim appears in TM Forum's published certification records as of early 2026. Most operator estates remain between L2 (partial autonomy) and L3 (conditional autonomy), and the peer-reviewed literature identifies why: trust in AI decisions, AI security, and data quality still gate the closed-loop pipeline from intent expression through assurance (Benzaid & Taleb, 2020; Leivadeas & Falkner, 2023; ETSI, 2024).

The strongest existence proof of software-defined operations at scale remains Rakuten Mobile's greenfield build: a fully cloud-native, containerized Open RAN which Rakuten reports as running more than 275,000 cells with roughly 250 operations staff — the scope of that staffing figure, and whether it excludes outsourced operations, is undefined in the source — executing up to one million methods-of-procedure daily through zero-touch orchestration (Rakuten Cloud, 2024). These figures are Rakuten-reported marketing numbers; independent measurement confirms competitive national-scale network performance but not the operational statistics (Opensignal, 2025).

### 2.3 AI-RAN: From Alliance Charter to One Outdoor Field Trial

The AI-RAN Alliance, launched at MWC 2024, defined three workstreams — AI-for-RAN, AI-and-RAN, and AI-on-RAN (AI-RAN Alliance, 2024) — and the O-RAN architecture supplies the control substrate through near-real-time and non-real-time RICs hosting xApps and rApps over open multi-vendor interfaces (Polese et al., 2023). What is demonstrated, as opposed to chartered: SoftBank's AITRAS, built on NVIDIA GH200, was validated in an outdoor field trial in Fujisawa City showing carrier-grade vRAN performance with concurrent AI inference multi-tenancy, with commercial deployment targeted from 2026 (SoftBank, 2024; NVIDIA, 2025) — a vendor-reported trial. SoftBank's operator blueprint claims approximately $5 of return per $1 invested from monetizing idle RAN compute, a self-reported projection rather than an audited result (SoftBank, 2024). Reinforcement-learning xApps for base-station energy saving have been demonstrated in peer-reviewed work with measurable savings and minimal user impact (Bordin et al., 2024). On the tooling side, NVIDIA's Aerial Omniverse Digital Twin is a shipping product: a 3GPP-compliant, ray-tracing RAN twin for city-scale simulation used to train and validate AI models before live deployment, integrated by Nokia, Keysight, VIAVI, Ansys, and AWS (NVIDIA, 2024). Network digital twins more broadly have crossed from research into formal standards work — ITU-T Y.3090 and the IRTF NMRG reference architecture (ITU, 2022; Zhou et al., 2025) — while surveys position the NDT as the mechanism for testing operational strategies without touching live networks (Almasan et al., 2022; Tran et al., 2025).

### 2.4 Quantum: What Runs, What Was Shown Once, What Does Not Exist

The quantum layer demands the most careful labeling. **Deployed:** quantum-inspired optimization runs in production — Fujitsu's Digital Annealer generates live production sequencing instructions at Toyota's Tsutsumi plant (Fujitsu & Toyota Systems, 2022), and the underlying quantum-inspired classical solvers demonstrably handle 100,000-variable problems on conventional hardware (Goto et al., 2021; Aramon et al., 2019). NIST finalized the first three PQC standards — ML-KEM, ML-DSA, SLH-DSA — in August 2024, giving operators concrete migration targets (NIST, 2024), with CNSA 2.0 setting category-specific exclusive-use deadlines through 2033 for national security systems (NSA, 2022/2025) and GSMA publishing the telco migration playbook (GSMA, 2023, 2024). QKD operates in metro production: BT and Toshiba's commercially operated London network since April 2022 (Lord et al., 2023), Madrid's 28-module SDN-managed MadQCI on live Telefónica fibre (Martín et al., 2024), and SK Telecom's multi-year carrier backbone deployment (ID Quantique, 2019–2024) — though critical analysis argues PQC delivers comparable guarantees at far lower cost for most metro use cases (Neppach et al., 2025).

**Demonstrated once, not sustained:** Volkswagen's Lisbon pilot put a D-Wave annealer in the loop for nine live buses during Web Summit 2019 (Yarkoni et al., 2020; Volkswagen Group, 2019); the Marunouchi waste-routing study projected a 2,300 km to ~1,000 km truck-distance reduction (D-Wave et al., 2020). Neither became a standing municipal service. **Not deployed, to the authors' knowledge and per the public benchmarking literature:** a fault-tolerant quantum processor in production. The honest benchmarking literature is unambiguous — current annealers and hybrid solvers fail to surpass Gurobi on many real problem classes (Pusey-Nazzaro et al., 2025), useful advantage in combinatorial optimization likely requires fault tolerance (Abbas et al., 2024), and classical decomposition and metaheuristics currently outperform quantum for system-level urban transport (Xu et al., 2026). The Mosca–Piani expert elicitation — subjective expert opinion, not a prediction — assigns a roughly one-in-three or higher aggregate probability to a cryptographically relevant quantum computer within the next decade, and a majority probability within fifteen years (Mosca & Piani, 2024) — which is exactly why the harvest-now-decrypt-later window, already open for long-lifetime data (Csenkey & Bindel, 2025; CISA et al., 2023), makes PQC migration the one quantum decision no city architect may defer.

The 2026 pattern across all four layers is consistent: standards and reference architectures are ahead of deployments, bounded L4 autonomy and quantum-inspired optimization are real, and the fully autonomous, quantum-accelerated city exists nowhere. The future is here — in single use cases, single districts, single fibre rings — and unevenly distributed.

## 3. From 2021 Vision to 2026 Reality: The 6G Ledger

Every generation of mobile technology begins with a vision paper, and every vision paper accumulates a ledger of predictions that reality subsequently audits. For 6G, the canonical entry is Tataria, Shafi, Molisch, Dohler, Sjöland, and Tufvesson (2021), who set out the defining directions of the sixth generation: terahertz-band operation approaching 1 THz, sub-millisecond end-to-end latency, flattened core network architectures, and cell-free deployment topologies. Its European counterpart, the Hexa-X flagship vision, framed 6G as the connective tissue between human, physical, and digital worlds, organized around six research challenges spanning connected intelligence, network of networks, sustainability, global coverage, extreme experience, and trustworthiness (Uusitalo et al., 2021). Five years later, with the ITU-R framework approved and 3GPP standardization underway, the ledger can be balanced with unusual precision. This section audits what was said against what is, and separates what remains possible from what has become probable — because the architecture decisions cities and operators make against this ledger will echo for decades.

### The framework that arrived: IMT-2030

The single most consequential event between the 2021 vision papers and the present is the approval of Recommendation ITU-R M.2160-0 in late 2023, which established the globally harmonized framework for IMT-2030 (International Telecommunication Union, 2023). The framework defines six usage scenarios — immersive communication, hyper-reliable and low-latency communication, massive communication, ubiquitous connectivity, AI-and-communication, and integrated sensing and communication — together with overarching design principles including sustainability and security/resilience. Two observations about this list matter for the ledger. First, three of the six scenarios extend 5G's familiar triangle of enhanced broadband, ultra-reliable low latency, and massive machine-type communication; continuity, not rupture, won the framework debate. Second, and more telling, the two genuinely new scenarios — AI-and-communication and integrated sensing and communication (ISAC) — were speculative research directions in 2021 and are now formal ITU commitments (International Telecommunication Union, 2023; Liu et al., 2022). The vision papers predicted capabilities; the framework canonized *functions*, and the functions it canonized were not the ones that dominated the 2021 headlines.

The standardization timeline, by contrast, landed almost exactly where Tataria et al. (2021) implied it would. The 6G technical study phase runs under Release 20, with study items approved at the June 2025 plenary and work commencing mid-2025, following the March 2025 Incheon workshop, which drew more than 600 attendees and 243 submitted contributions (Ericsson, 2025). Normative 6G work is carried by Release 21, whose approved schedule places the first 6G functional freeze in December 2028, with ASN.1 freeze no earlier than March 2029 and commercial systems targeted around 2030 (3GPP, 2025). The decade-scale standardization cadence held; what changed inside the cadence is the substance.

### THz: from headline to footnote

The most visible correction in the ledger concerns spectrum. Tataria et al. (2021) devoted substantial attention to bands up to roughly 1 THz, while candidly flagging the range-versus-rate tradeoff that terahertz propagation imposes. That caveat carried the most weight in hindsight. By 2023–2024, industry consensus had recalibrated decisively toward the upper mid-band: the 7–24 GHz FR3 range emerged as the pragmatic 6G workhorse, inheriting the coverage economics of sub-6 GHz and the capacity headroom of millimeter wave (Cui et al., 2023). Ericsson's spectrum position is representative of the vendor community: the centimetric 7–15 GHz band is essential for wide-area 6G capacity, with roughly 2.4 GHz of suitable spectrum required, while sub-THz operation is explicitly relegated to a complementary role (Ericsson, 2024).

This is not to say the THz prediction failed outright; it was deprioritized rather than disproven. In April 2024, NTT DOCOMO, NTT, NEC, and Fujitsu demonstrated 100 Gbps transmission in the 100 GHz and 300 GHz sub-terahertz bands over distances up to 100 m — approximately twenty times current 5G peak rates (NTT DOCOMO et al., 2024). That result is precisely what the 2021 physics predicted: extraordinary rates over short ranges, suitable for niche extreme-capacity "data pipes" rather than city-wide coverage. The ledger entry reads: *possible* was demonstrated; *probable* moved down-band. For city-scale digital twin architects, the practical consequence is that 6G's urban sensing and connectivity fabric will ride on FR3 propagation characteristics, not THz ones — a materially different picture for antenna density, indoor penetration, and infrastructure siting than the 2021 literature implied.

### The headline that replaced it: AI-native operation

What displaced THz at the top of the agenda was barely present in the 2021 requirements tables: the AI-native air interface. Hoydis, Aoudia, Valcarce, and Viswanathan (2021) articulated the proposition in the same year as the Tataria vision — an air interface partially designed by machine learning rather than hand-engineered, noting pointedly that no 5G component had been ML-designed. Between 2021 and 2026 this progressed from proposition to trajectory: "AI and Communication" became a formal IMT-2030 usage scenario (International Telecommunication Union, 2023), and the Hexa-X-II final system evaluation validated AI-native air interfaces, joint communication and sensing, and intent-driven orchestration with cognitive closed loops at proof-of-concept level, showcased at EuCNC & 6G Summit 2025 (Hexa-X-II Consortium, 2025). ISAC followed a parallel trajectory from foundational survey to framework pillar: dual-functional radar-communication waveform design, formalized by Liu et al. (2022), now carries the implication that the network itself becomes a city-scale sensor — a development of direct architectural significance for urban digital twins, since it collapses the boundary between the connectivity layer and the observation layer.

Whether AI-native design delivers on the air interface itself remains an open question; over-the-air trials exist, but no normative 3GPP release yet specifies ML-designed physical-layer components, and the Release 20 study phase will determine how far the concept penetrates the Release 21 specifications (3GPP, 2025; Ericsson, 2025). What is no longer open is the direction of travel.

### The design tool nobody headlined: the network digital twin

The quietest entry in the ledger may prove the most durable. The 2021 vision papers treated simulation as background tooling; by 2026, the network digital twin (NDT) had become a first-class architectural object. The trajectory is traceable through three registers. In standards, ITU-T Y.3090 established the first international digital-twin-network recommendation in February 2022, defining a three-layer architecture for real-time interactive mapping between physical and twin networks (International Telecommunication Union, 2022), while the IRTF Network Management Research Group advanced a reference architecture standardizing NDT concepts for data-driven network management (Zhou et al., 2025). In research, the NDT was defined as a real-time, data-driven model for what-if analysis and closed-loop optimization (Almasan et al., 2022), and by 2025 comprehensive surveys spanned RAN, transport, core, cloud/edge, and non-terrestrial domains, positioning the NDT as the mechanism for testing designs and fault scenarios without disturbing live networks (Tran et al., 2025). In products, GPU ray-tracing-based RAN twins now support site-specific, city-scale simulation of hundreds of base stations and thousands of user devices, explicitly framed as the training-and-validation loop for AI-native algorithms before live deployment (NVIDIA, 2024). On this trajectory, the 6G that arrives around 2030 may become the first generation substantially *designed inside its own digital twin* — a labeled projection, since no evidence yet shows NDTs formally embedded in the Release 21 design process, but one with obvious resonance for this whitepaper's central argument that telecom twins and city twins must interoperate rather than merely coexist.

### Balancing the ledger

Read as a whole, the 2021-to-2026 audit yields a consistent pattern. The predictions grounded in physics held, including their caveats: sub-THz works exactly as far as the link budget said it would (NTT DOCOMO et al., 2024). The predictions grounded in institutional cadence held: standardization is running on the decade clock (3GPP, 2025). The predictions that missed were predictions of *emphasis* — which capability would headline the generation — and the corrections all moved in one direction: away from raw radio exotica, toward intelligence, sensing, and simulation as the defining substance of 6G (International Telecommunication Union, 2023; Hexa-X-II Consortium, 2025). Sub-millisecond latency and core flattening remain in the requirements conversation but await the Release 20 studies and Release 21 normative work for their binding form; they sit today in the *possible* column rather than the *probable* one. The lesson for the city architect is uncomfortable but clear: the components of a generational vision most likely to survive are not its most spectacular claims but its structural ones — and the structural claim now hardening into standards is that the network of 2030 will observe, model, and rehearse itself continuously. The next section examines what happens when that capability meets the autonomous-operations frameworks already certifying real networks today.

## 4. Self-Healing Networks and TM Forum Alignment

### 4.1 The Autonomy Ladder as the City's Operational Backbone

Any credible discussion of a self-governing urban infrastructure must begin with the maturity instrument the telecommunications industry has already built for exactly this purpose. TM Forum's Autonomous Networks framework defines six levels of network autonomy, modeled deliberately on the autonomous-vehicle taxonomy: from manual operation (L0), through assisted (L1), partial (L2), conditional (L3), and highly autonomous (L4) operation, to full autonomy (L5) across all services, domains, and lifecycle phases. The framework and business requirements are specified in IG1218 (TM Forum, IG1218, 2021); the operational definitions and grading criteria of each level — per operational task, across execution, awareness, analysis, decision, and intent/experience — are specified in the levels-evaluation methodology (TM Forum, IG1252, 2021, and later versions). The companion technical architecture organizes this ambition into Autonomous Domains — bounded operational scopes that self-manage through closed control loops of awareness, analysis, decision, and execution, with intent managers mediating between domains rather than exposing internal complexity upward (TM Forum, IG1230, 2022). This is the load-bearing abstraction for the quantum city: the domain boundary is where a building twin, a grid twin, and a RAN twin negotiate, and the closed loop is the unit of self-healing.

Critically, the L0–L5 scale has crossed from maturity narrative into auditable measurement. The IG1252 evaluation methodology, together with TM Forum's autonomous-network certification and assessment program, formalizes how an operator's autonomy claims are assessed and certified (TM Forum, IG1252, 2021, and later versions), and Huawei reports that a first cohort of operators received formal level certificates in late 2024 (Huawei, 2024) — a vendor-reported milestone. The results since then come chiefly from operator reporting rather than laboratory work. China Mobile has documented a Level 4 network operation center driven by intelligent agents at national scale (TM Forum Inform, 2025). Rakuten Mobile reported the first TM Forum validation of a Level 4 use case in a live commercial Open RAN network — RAN energy-efficiency optimization yielding operator-reported savings of roughly 20% in production (Rakuten Mobile, 2026). Telefónica reported twelve Level 4 use cases across Spain, Brazil, and Germany at the close of 2025, audited quarterly per domain and process (Telefónica, 2026). Pending confirmation against TM Forum's certification records, the implication for a city architect is nonetheless direct: closed-loop machinery of the kind that will orchestrate urban digital twins already operates at L4 for bounded use cases in commercial networks, and its maturity is measured against a published methodology rather than merely asserted.

### 4.2 Intent, ODA, and the Grammar of Cross-Domain Delegation

Self-healing at city scale is a delegation problem before it is an algorithm problem. IG1253 defines intent as the formal specification of all expectations — including requirements, goals, and constraints — given to a technical system, together with the lifecycle management of intent objects: creation, negotiation, reporting, removal (TM Forum, IG1253, 2022; see also the intent common and extension models, IG1253A–C, where the intent ontology is relied upon). The load-bearing point of that definition is that intents are machine-interpretable specifications, not merely communications. TMF921 operationalizes this as a standardized API: intent owners set intents, intent handlers report compliance, and the two negotiate feasibility before commitment (TM Forum, TMF921, 2024). The peer-reviewed literature converges on the same pipeline — intent expression, translation, resolution, activation, and continuous assurance form the closed-loop automation chain, with assurance closing the loop back to expression when the network drifts from the declared goal (Leivadeas & Falkner, 2023). ETSI's zero-touch (ZSM) work targets the same end state of fully automated operational processes, while candidly cataloguing the AI trust, security, and data-quality challenges that still gate full autonomy (Benzaid & Taleb, 2020; ETSI, 2024).

The Open Digital Architecture supplies the composition substrate: ODA components are self-contained, independently deployable, plug-and-play modules exposing functionality through standardized, discoverable APIs — TM Forum Open APIs wherever such APIs are defined (TM Forum, IG1171, 2024). The structural resonance with the Digital Twin Consortium's Composability Framework — composable digital twins assembled from packaged business capabilities (Digital Twin Consortium, 2024) — suggests broad industry consensus on the principle, though the two bodies are not independent witnesses: cross-membership is common and this paper itself proposes joint TM Forum–DTC work items in Section 7. A city that adopts ODA-style componentry for its twin services gains access to the intent grammar through which — in a hypothetical scenario, since no city has yet demonstrated cross-administrative-domain intent negotiation (see Research Agenda item 4) — a flood-response twin could tell a network domain "guarantee 50 ms latency to these 400 sensors for the next six hours" and receive a feasibility answer rather than a trouble ticket. Adopting that grammar entails real integration, semantic-mapping, and governance cost; what the componentry buys is the possibility, not the deployment.

### 4.3 Closing the Loop Twice: AN Control Loops and the Grieves Cycle

The TM Forum closed loop (awareness–analysis–decision–execution) and the digital twin loop are the same loop observed from two professions. Grieves's Front Running Simulation framework decomposes twin operation into replicate, predict, and navigate (Grieves, 2023): replication corresponds to awareness (the twin ingests state), prediction to analysis (the twin runs faster than real time to see failure before it arrives), and navigation to decision and execution (the twin selects and enacts the intervention). Network digital twins make this correspondence concrete: the NDT is defined precisely as a real-time, data-driven model of the network used for what-if analysis and closed-loop optimization (Almasan et al., 2022), standardized by ITU-T Y.3090's three-layer architecture for real-time interactive mapping between physical and twin networks (International Telecommunication Union, 2022) and now progressing through IRTF standards-track architecture work (Zhou et al., 2025). Recent survey work extends the NDT across RAN, transport, core, edge, and security domains as the mechanism for rehearsing fault scenarios without touching the live network (Tran et al., 2025). Self-healing, in this framing, is Front Running Simulation executed by an L4 autonomous domain: the twin front-runs the fault, and the intent interface authorizes the correction.

### 4.4 The Near-Term Substrate: AI-RAN and the RAN Digital Twin

The most concrete self-healing substrate available today sits in the radio access network. The O-RAN architecture provides the actuation points: the near-real-time RIC hosts xApps on 10 ms–1 s control loops and the non-real-time RIC hosts rApps above 1 s, enabling data-driven closed-loop control over open, multi-vendor interfaces (Polese et al., 2023). Demonstrated reinforcement-learning xApps already switch RF frontends dynamically for measurable energy savings with minimal user impact (Bordin et al., 2024) — the same use-case family Rakuten carried to its reported L4 validation (Rakuten Mobile, 2026), atop a cloud-native operational base Rakuten reports as executing up to one million methods-of-procedure per day with roughly 250 operations staff for 275,000+ cells (Rakuten Cloud, 2024; Opensignal, 2025). The AI-RAN Alliance's three workstreams (AI-for-RAN, AI-and-RAN, AI-on-RAN) formalize the convergence of RAN and AI workloads on shared accelerated infrastructure (AI-RAN Alliance, 2024), with SoftBank's AITRAS field trial demonstrating carrier-grade vRAN and concurrent AI inference on shared GPUs outdoors in Fujisawa City (SoftBank Corp., 2024; NVIDIA, 2025), and proposed orchestration extensions to the O-RAN SMO to manage both workload classes jointly (Polese et al., 2025). The training-and-validation half of the loop is supplied by city-scale RAN digital twins: ray-traced, 3GPP-compliant simulation of hundreds of base stations and thousands of UEs to validate AI models before they touch the live network (NVIDIA, 2024) — Grieves's "predict" stage, industrialized.

### 4.5 Where Quantum Enters — and the Honest Boundary

Within this architecture, quantum computation has one plausible seat: inside the analysis-and-decision stage of the closed loop, where combinatorial decision problems must be solved inside collapsing time windows. Many of these problems — correlating anomaly matrices across domains during cascade failures, reassigning spectrum and compute after a fault, re-routing service chains — can be expressed in QUBO form, the formulation quantum annealers and QAOA-class algorithms consume (Farhi et al., 2014). The candid state of the evidence, however, must anchor any TM Forum architect's planning. Authoritative assessments conclude that useful quantum advantage in combinatorial optimization likely requires fault tolerance and approximation-hard instances, and that most near-term claims do not survive fair classical benchmarking (Abbas et al., 2024); current annealing hybrids frequently fail to beat Gurobi on real problem classes (Pusey-Nazzaro et al., 2025), and advantage claims themselves are fraught to establish (Codognet et al., 2025). What is deployable today is quantum-inspired classical computation: simulated-bifurcation machines solving 100,000-variable Ising problems on GPUs and FPGAs (Goto et al., 2019; Goto et al., 2021), digital annealers benchmarking competitively on dense QUBO instances (Aramon et al., 2019) and running in live production sequencing at Toyota (Fujitsu Limited & Toyota Systems Corporation, 2022). This is the QI stage of the DTC maturity model in operational fact, and it maps onto AI-RAN economics: the same shared GPU pool that hosts inference workloads (SoftBank Corp., 2024) can host QUBO solvers as rApps today. Whether a quantum processing unit ever joins the near-RT loop is an open question — and so is whether QUBO is the right standard interface for the L4 closed loop. QUBO was designed for annealer compatibility and often carries encoding overhead and penalty-weight fragility relative to native MILP or constraint-programming formulations, and the same benchmarking literature that favors classical solvers cuts against premature standardization on an annealer-shaped abstraction. The honest position is that QUBO is a candidate abstraction worth piloting behind a solver-neutral interface, not a settled architectural commitment.

## 5. The Quantum City: Where Quantum Actually Fits

The word "quantum" attracts more budget distortion than any other term in the smart-city lexicon. The Digital Twin Consortium's Quantum Compute and Operations maturity stages — quantum-aware, quantum-inspired, quantum-resilient, hybrid quantum-classical, and quantum-native — exist precisely to discipline that distortion: each stage carries a distinct time horizon, a distinct procurement decision, and a distinct falsifiable benchmark. This section maps those stages onto four horizons — next week, next month to next year, next years, and next decade — and states plainly what the evidence supports at each. The honest headline is that the most urgent quantum decision facing a city architect today involves no quantum computer at all.

### Next week: post-quantum cryptography and the harvest-now-decrypt-later clock

The one quantum workstream with a deadline that has already passed is cryptographic migration. An adversary who passively records encrypted traffic today can decrypt it retroactively once a cryptographically relevant quantum computer exists; for data generated from 2020 onward with confidentiality requirements of ten years or more, the risk window is already open (Csenkey & Bindel, 2025). The Mosca–Piani expert elicitation — subjective expert opinion, not a prediction — assigns a roughly one-in-three or higher aggregate probability to a cryptographically relevant quantum computer within the next decade, and a majority probability within fifteen years (Mosca & Piani, 2024). City digital twins are an unusually attractive harvest target: building access logs, occupancy patterns, utility telemetry, and health-adjacent environmental data all retain sensitivity for decades — longer than the expected life of the encryption protecting them in transit.

The remediation path is standardized. NIST finalized ML-KEM, ML-DSA, and SLH-DSA as FIPS 203, 204, and 205 in August 2024 (National Institute of Standards and Technology, 2024), joint U.S. government guidance directs critical-infrastructure operators to complete cryptographic inventories and vendor engagement now (Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency et al., 2023), and CNSA 2.0 sets category-specific exclusive-use deadlines through 2033 for national security systems — software and firmware signing by 2030, networking equipment by 2030–2033, browsers, servers, and cloud services by 2033 — with the 2035 horizon for all federal and national security systems deriving from NSM-10-aligned migration guidance rather than CNSA 2.0 itself (National Security Agency, 2022/2025). On the telecom side that city twins depend on, the GSMA task force has published both a domain-by-domain impact assessment covering SIM, transport, core, and PKI (GSMA Post Quantum Telco Network Task Force, 2023) and phased migration guidelines for operator use cases (GSMA Post Quantum Telco Network Task Force, 2024); preprint benchmarking further indicates ML-KEM and ML-DSA perform practically in the specific 6G-candidate network procedures tested (Toward Quantum-Safe 6G, 2026, preprint — an unreviewed result, cited as such). The architectural principle that matters more than any single algorithm is crypto-agility: cryptographic primitives must be swappable configuration, not compiled-in assumptions, because FIPS 203–205 will not be the last migration. Quantum key distribution, by contrast, remains a niche: operational metro deployments exist in London, Madrid, and Seoul (Lord et al., 2023; Martín et al., 2024; ID Quantique, 2019–2024), but critical analysis finds that for most metro and telecom use cases PQC delivers comparable security at far lower cost and complexity (Neppach et al., 2025). Cities should mandate PQC and treat QKD as an option to monitor, not a requirement to procure.

### Next month to next year: quantum-inspired optimization, benchmark-gated

The quantum-inspired stage requires no quantum hardware and is already in production elsewhere. Fujitsu's Digital Annealer runs live production sequencing at Toyota's Tsutsumi plant (Fujitsu Limited & Toyota Systems Corporation, 2022), and the underlying physics-inspired solver families — digital annealing and simulated bifurcation — handle dense QUBO instances up to 100,000 variables with competitive-to-superior wall-clock performance against conventional heuristics (Aramon et al., 2019; Goto et al., 2019; Goto et al., 2021). This is the maturity stage of the DTC Q-POD testbed, and the city-relevant problem classes are well mapped: traffic routing, demonstrated in the Volkswagen–D-Wave Lisbon pilot that routed nine Carris buses in near real time on live traffic data for one week (Yarkoni et al., 2020; Volkswagen Group, 2019); traffic-signal control formulated as lattice QUBO (Inoue et al., 2021); and waste collection, where the Marunouchi pilot combined demand prediction with annealing and projected a truck-distance reduction from roughly 2,300 km to 1,000 km (D-Wave Systems et al., 2020).

The discipline is in the gate, not the pilot. A critical assessment of quantum optimization in urban transport finds classical decomposition, metaheuristics, and reinforcement learning currently outperform quantum approaches at system scale, with the near-term quantum niche confined to vulnerability analysis, failure-scenario screening, and small facility-location problems (Xu et al., 2026). Limitation studies show current annealers remain size-constrained and hybrid solvers frequently fail to beat Gurobi on time or quality (Pusey-Nazzaro et al., 2025), and the authoritative field assessment concludes that useful quantum advantage in combinatorial optimization likely requires fault tolerance and approximation-hard problem structure (Abbas et al., 2024). Every quantum or quantum-inspired claim must therefore clear a classical baseline on the city's own data before adoption — for waste routing, the published metaheuristic baselines for real municipal instances provide exactly such a yardstick (Silva et al., 2023).

### Next years: hybrid QPU calls at portfolio scale

The hybrid stage — classical orchestration with selective QPU subroutine calls — has credible demonstrations at the boundary of city relevance. A hybrid quantum-classical solver has handled a real package-delivery routing problem with heterogeneous fleets, priorities, and dual capacity constraints on annealing hardware (Osaba et al., 2024), and an IonQ–Oak Ridge National Laboratory collaboration ran a hybrid decomposition approach to power-grid unit commitment on trapped-ion hardware, with classical solvers still leading at scale (Aboumrad et al., 2025). Water-network work remains at seven-node toy instances (Romero Rocha et al., 2025) — an honest calibration point. The plausible entry problems for cities are portfolio-scale: joint optimization across a district energy system, a fleet of fifty or more buildings, or a metro network, where problem structure is dense, coupled, and periodically re-solved. Whether QPU calls beat mature classical decomposition at those scales within this horizon is an open question; the architecture decision is to ensure the twin's optimization layer can issue the call when the benchmark says yes. For the grid domain, only conceptual frameworks — paper architectures, not implementations — exist for slotting quantum modules into classical digital twin stacks; no quantum module has been integrated into any operating grid twin (Lemo et al., 2025). The broader mapping of quantum computing, sensing, and communication onto smart-city domains is peer-reviewed (Bashirpour Bonab et al., 2023).

### Next decade: quantum-native simulation for front-running twins

The quantum-native horizon is projection, clearly labeled as such. The theoretical foundation is real: amplitude estimation offers a near-quadratic speedup over classical Monte Carlo (Montanaro, 2015), has been applied to risk measures such as value-at-risk (Woerner & Egger, 2019), and NISQ-friendly iterative variants have drastically reduced circuit-depth requirements (Grinko et al., 2021). If a city twin of 2046 continuously front-runs reality — simulating storm response, evacuation, or grid contingency ensembles faster than events unfold — quantum-accelerated uncertainty quantification is the natural fit, because ensemble Monte Carlo is precisely where the quadratic advantage bites. But the field's own consensus counsels restraint: expected speedups for many problem classes vanish under realistic input/output and error-correction overheads (Mandrà et al., 2024), and establishing advantage is fraught both technically and sociologically (Codognet et al., 2025). Cities should treat quantum-native simulation as a decade-scale option to preserve, not a capability to procure.

### The architectural through-line: the solver swaps, the data stays

One principle unifies all four horizons. If the twin's optimization interface is a well-defined problem formulation — a QUBO, a unit-commitment instance, a routing matrix — then classical heuristic, quantum-inspired annealer, hybrid QPU pipeline, and eventual quantum-native solver become interchangeable backends. The problem travels to the solver; the data stays sovereign at the edge, with only formulated problem instances and differentially private aggregates crossing the boundary. This is the same composability thesis the DTC framework formalizes for digital twin capabilities generally (Digital Twin Consortium, 2024), and it converts quantum uncertainty from an existential architecture risk into a procurement option. A city that hardcodes today's solver rebuilds its twin when the hardware shifts. A city that builds crypto-agile, solver-agnostic, edge-sovereign infrastructure makes exactly one decision now — the interface — and lets the next twenty years of quantum results decide the rest.

## 6. Architecture That Echoes: SMILE, MIMs, and the Sovereign Stack

The preceding sections established two empirical patterns: quantum and quantum-inspired optimization delivers value today only where problems can be cleanly encoded and shipped to a solver (Yarkoni et al., 2020; Fujitsu Limited & Toyota Systems Corporation, 2022), and autonomous network operation reaches Level 4 only where closed loops, intent interfaces, and componentized architecture were designed in from the start (TM Forum, IG1230, 2022; Rakuten Mobile, 2026). This section draws the normative consequence. It specifies a reference stack for the quantum-ready city and argues that its defining properties — minimal interoperability, composability, data sovereignty, and post-quantum cryptography on every link — are architectural commitments that are far cheaper to make at design time than to retrofit. The evidence for the difficulty of late correction is already in the literature: systematic reviews of urban digital twins consistently identify interoperability and data governance, not modeling technology, as the dominant blockers to operational deployment (Lei et al., 2023; Mazzetto, 2024).

### 6.1 A five-plane edge-native reference stack

We propose the reference stack as five planes. The *sensing plane* comprises physical instrumentation and, increasingly, the network itself: integrated sensing and communication elevates the radio infrastructure to a city-scale sensor, now formalized as an IMT-2030 usage scenario (Liu et al., 2022; International Telecommunication Union, 2023). The *connectivity plane* carries observations and control with deterministic latency; latency-critical urban workloads — traffic control, video analytics, utility actuation — demonstrably require processing at the edge rather than round-trips to centralized clouds (Khan et al., 2020). The *edge plane* hosts local inference, local actuation, and local model refinement; as an illustrative design pattern for smart buildings, incremental learning models execute on-premises and only differentially private aggregates leave the building — a pattern this paper proposes as a design target rather than cites as deployed evidence, since no published evaluation of such deployments is available to reference. The *twin plane* maintains the synchronized, semantically structured representation of physical assets, following the ISO/IEC 30173 definitional baseline (International Organization for Standardization & International Electrotechnical Commission, 2023), CityGML 3.0 semantics for the urban fabric (Kutzner et al., 2020; Open Geospatial Consortium, 2021), and ITU-T Y.4600 capability requirements for city-scale twins (International Telecommunication Union, 2022). The *solver plane* is deliberately last and deliberately detachable: it receives problem encodings — QUBO formulations, unit-commitment instances, routing constraints — and returns solutions, whether from classical metaheuristics (Silva et al., 2023), quantum-inspired hardware (Goto et al., 2019; Aramon et al., 2019), annealers (Osaba et al., 2024), or gate-model hybrids (Aboumrad et al., 2025). Because the near-term balance between classical and quantum solvers remains unsettled (Abbas et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2026), the architecture must treat the solver as a swappable service behind a stable encoding interface, exactly as the DTC quantum maturity model's QA-through-QN progression implies.

### 6.2 Minimal interoperability, maximal composability

Two governance instruments make this stack federable rather than monolithic. The first is minimal interoperability. The OASC MIMs Plus specifications define the smallest common set of mechanisms — context information management, shared data models, security, personal-data handling — that permit city-to-city and city-to-supplier interoperability without mandating identical implementations across the 150+ participating sub-national governments (Open & Agile Smart Cities & Living-in.EU Technical Sub-Group, 2024). This is the correct granularity for cities: agreement on interfaces, sovereignty over implementations. The second is composability. The Digital Twin Consortium's Capabilities Periodic Table provides an architecture-agnostic requirements vocabulary across six capability clusters (Digital Twin Consortium, 2022), and the Composability Framework combines it with the Platform Stack Architectural Framework so that twins are assembled from packaged business capabilities rather than built as bespoke silos (Digital Twin Consortium, 2024). The same pattern appears in adjacent standards: ISO 23247 gives manufacturing twins a layered reference framework with per-asset digital identity that NIST analysis shows generalizes across discrete, batch, and continuous production (International Organization for Standardization, 2021; Shao et al., 2023), and ITU-T Y.4489 standardizes federation of independent twins through registries and communication adaptors (International Telecommunication Union, 2023). On the network side, TM Forum's ODA components — self-contained, independently deployable modules exposing functionality through standardized, discoverable APIs, TM Forum Open APIs wherever defined (TM Forum, IG1171, 2024) — and intent objects as formal specifications of expectations, goals, and constraints (TM Forum, IG1253, 2022) provide the corresponding composability substrate for the connectivity plane. The convergence of these frameworks on the same principle — small interfaces, replaceable parts — suggests broad industry consensus, though it should not be mistaken for independent validation: the bodies involved share members and collaborate, including in the joint agenda this paper proposes in Section 7.

### 6.3 SMILE: querying reality through its representation

Interoperable planes still require a coherent interaction paradigm. The SMILE methodology — a methodology previously published by the author (Waern, 2025), and the answer returns either from reality itself — live sensor state, actuator confirmation — or from continuously fused sensor, human, contextual, and simulation data. Queries traverse time bidirectionally: backward into recorded state, forward into simulated state, consistent with Grieves's (2023) replicate–predict–navigate framing of front-running simulation, and they zoom across spatial scales from component to building to district. The framing depends on capabilities the stack described above provides. Temporal traversal requires the versioning and time-dependent properties CityGML 3.0 introduced expressly for twin applications (Kutzner et al., 2020); forward traversal requires simulation-before-implementation capability of the kind ITU-T Y.4600 mandates (International Telecommunication Union, 2022); and answer latency requires edge-resident computation (Khan et al., 2020). Where a forward query encodes an optimization — "which signal timing minimizes flow imbalance across this district?" — the solver plane answers it, whether by quantum annealing (Inoue et al., 2021) or classical baseline (Silva et al., 2023). SMILE is thus neither a product claim nor validation of the stack; it is a proposed discipline: every capability in the stack exists to answer a question posed to reality through its representation.

### 6.4 Data stays home; insights and encodings travel

The sovereignty rule of the stack is simple to state: raw data remains at the heart of the building, hospital, or city that generated it; what travels are insights, differentially private aggregates, and problem encodings. This is technically feasible — the Gaia-X/FIWARE federation demonstrates sovereignty-preserving integration of transport, energy, water, and lighting data across trust domains (Cirillo et al., 2024) — and it is normatively grounded in the Gemini Principles' pairing of openness with security and federation with curation (Bolton et al., 2018). It is also what makes the solver plane safe: a QUBO matrix shipped to an external annealer discloses problem structure, not personal trajectories.

Every inter-plane and inter-domain link must carry post-quantum cryptography from first deployment. NIST finalized ML-KEM, ML-DSA, and SLH-DSA in August 2024 (National Institute of Standards and Technology, 2024); CNSA 2.0 sets category-specific exclusive-use deadlines through 2033, with the 2035 target for all national security systems set by NSM-10-aligned federal migration guidance (National Security Agency, 2022/2025); and GSMA guidance operationalizes the migration for telecom domains (GSMA Post Quantum Telco Network Task Force, 2024). The reason PQC belongs in the foundation rather than a later patch is temporal: harvest-now-decrypt-later means ciphertext recorded today can be decrypted retroactively, and for data with decade-plus confidentiality lifetimes — health records, building occupancy, grid telemetry — the risk window is already open (Csenkey & Bindel, 2025), with the Mosca–Piani expert elicitation assigning a roughly one-in-three or higher aggregate probability to a cryptographically relevant quantum computer within the next decade (Mosca & Piani, 2024). Preprint benchmarking indicates PQC performs practically in the specific 6G-candidate network procedures tested (Toward Quantum-Safe 6G, 2026, preprint), and for most metro use cases PQC delivers comparable assurance to QKD at far lower cost (Neppach et al., 2025).

### 6.5 Why retrofit costs compound

Each of these decisions is cheap at design time and substantially more expensive afterward — with one category-specific exception that proves the rule. Semantic models cannot be layered onto twins built from unstructured geometry without re-acquisition; sovereignty is hard to reintroduce once raw data flows have centralized and dependencies have formed around them; PQC cannot protect traffic already harvested, which is why cryptographic migration — itself a retrofit program, as the CNSA 2.0 and GSMA timelines demonstrate — is feasible for future traffic but powerless over the past; and a solver interface cannot be abstracted out of code that hard-wired one optimizer's formulation without significant re-engineering. Quantified retrofit-cost studies for city-twin infrastructure do not yet exist, and this paper does not claim retrofit is impossible — only that the urban digital twin literature documents the resulting lock-in in kind: heterogeneous integration failures and governance gaps dominate deployment barriers precisely because they were not architectural commitments at inception (Lei et al., 2023; Nochta et al., 2021). Whether the solver plane's 2046 workload is quantum-native remains an open question; that the interfaces around it are far cheaper to lay now is not.

## 7. Conclusion and Research Agenda

The three cities of this whitepaper — the city of legacy silos, the city under construction in 2026, and the city of 2046 — do not converge by default. They converge or diverge based on architecture decisions being made now, most of which appear administrative rather than strategic at the moment of signing. The evidence assembled in the preceding sections supports a specific reading of the present. Autonomous network operation has crossed from maturity narrative to auditable practice: the L0–L5 scale defined in TM Forum's framework (TM Forum, IG1218, 2021) is now a measurement and certification instrument (TM Forum, IG1252, 2021, and later versions; Huawei, 2024), with Level 4 use cases reported by operators in live commercial networks (Rakuten Mobile, 2026; Telefónica, 2026) and in national-scale operations centers (TM Forum Inform, 2025). Quantum-inspired optimization runs in production instruction at automotive scale (Fujitsu Limited & Toyota Systems Corporation, 2022), and a pilot study projected meaningful urban impact in waste routing, though it never became a standing service (D-Wave Systems et al., 2020). Post-quantum cryptography has moved from conjecture to finalized federal standards (National Institute of Standards and Technology, 2024) with hard transition deadlines already published (National Security Agency, 2022/2025). At the same time, the honest counterweights remain in force: classical solvers still outperform quantum approaches for most system-level urban optimization (Xu et al., 2026; Abbas et al., 2024), city-twin interoperability remains the dominant deployment blocker (Lei et al., 2023; Mazzetto, 2024), and published outcome-level KPIs for flagship city twins are conspicuously scarce (Singapore Land Authority / National Research Foundation, n.d.).

The divergence scenario is therefore not a failure of technology but a failure of interface discipline. A city that procures its network twin, its building twins, and its optimization services as vertically integrated, cryptographically legacy, schema-proprietary systems in 2026 will find in 2046 that each subsystem works and none of them compose — the precise condition documented in today's urban digital twin literature (Lei et al., 2023; Nochta et al., 2021). The convergence scenario requires that the interfaces now being standardized in parallel — intent (TM Forum, IG1253, 2022; TM Forum, TMF921, 2024), ODA components (TM Forum, IG1171, 2024), network digital twin architecture (Zhou et al., 2025; International Telecommunication Union, 2022), city twin federation (International Telecommunication Union, 2023), and composable twin capabilities (Digital Twin Consortium, 2024) — be treated as a single architectural fabric rather than as parallel silo standards. That treatment does not yet exist. Creating it is the research agenda.

We propose five concrete work items, each suited to joint TM Forum–Digital Twin Consortium execution.

**First, a solver-neutral optimization problem-interchange schema, with QUBO as a candidate to be evaluated rather than presumed.** The urban optimization results surveyed here — traffic routing (Yarkoni et al., 2020), signal control (Inoue et al., 2021), logistics (Osaba et al., 2024), waste collection (D-Wave Systems et al., 2020) — all reduce to QUBO or closely related formulations, yet each was encoded against a specific solver stack, and QUBO's encoding overhead and penalty-weight fragility relative to native MILP and constraint-programming formulations remain open questions (see Section 4.5). The cross-domain solver portability thesis of the DTC Quantum Compute and Operations working group requires an interchange format in which a city twin can express an optimization problem once and dispatch it to quantum annealers, gate-model QAOA implementations (Farhi et al., 2014; Blekos et al., 2024), quantum-inspired classical engines (Goto et al., 2019; Aramon et al., 2019), or native classical solvers without re-encoding. Such a schema also enables the fair, like-for-like benchmarking that the quantum optimization literature repeatedly identifies as missing (Abbas et al., 2024; Codognet et al., 2025) — including benchmarking of the QUBO abstraction itself.

**Second, PQC profiles for digital twin protocols.** GSMA has produced impact assessments and migration guidelines for telecom network domains (GSMA Post Quantum Telco Network Task Force, 2023, 2024), and national blueprints for quantum-secure mobile cores exist (Telecommunication Engineering Centre, 2025). No equivalent profile exists for twin-to-twin protocols: the federation interfaces of ITU-T Y.4489, the context-brokerage layers of city data platforms (Cirillo et al., 2024), or the intent APIs of TMF921. City infrastructure data carries confidentiality lifetimes measured in decades, which places it squarely inside the harvest-now-decrypt-later risk window that opened for long-lived data years ago (Csenkey & Bindel, 2025; Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency et al., 2023). The Mosca–Piani expert elicitation assigns a roughly one-in-three or higher aggregate probability to a cryptographically relevant quantum computer within the next decade — well within the operating life of infrastructure commissioned today (Mosca & Piani, 2024). A joint task force should specify ML-KEM and ML-DSA profiles (National Institute of Standards and Technology, 2024) for each twin interoperability standard, with the QKD-versus-PQC cost analysis of Neppach et al. (2025) as a scoping input.

**Third, benchmark and source-quality disclosure standards for quantum and quantum-inspired claims.** The gap between deployed evidence (Fujitsu Limited & Toyota Systems Corporation, 2022; Volkswagen Group, 2019) and unverifiable projection is currently policed only by the diligence of individual readers, and — as this paper's own audit shows — many load-bearing figures in the field originate in vendor and operator press materials rather than peer-reviewed or independently audited sources. The optimization community has documented how advantage claims dissolve under fair classical baselines (Willsch et al., 2020; Pusey-Nazzaro et al., 2025; Mandrà et al., 2024). We propose a disclosure template — problem size, encoding overhead, classical baseline and its tuning effort, wall-clock and quality metrics, and source-quality tier — modeled on the auditable rigor TM Forum applied to autonomy levels (TM Forum, IG1252, 2021, and later versions). If autonomy claims can be certified, solver claims can be too.

**Fourth, city-twin and autonomous-network convergence via intent.** The intent construct (TM Forum, IG1253, 2022; Leivadeas & Falkner, 2023) is the natural boundary object between a municipal twin expressing goals ("reduce corridor congestion," "hold district energy within budget") and autonomous network domains executing closed loops beneath it (TM Forum, IG1230, 2022; Benzaid & Taleb, 2020). Hexa-X-II has demonstrated intent-driven orchestration with cognitive closed loops at PoC level (Hexa-X-II Consortium, 2025); what remains open — and what the hypothetical flood-response scenario of Section 4.2 presupposes — is whether TMF921-style intent negotiation can span the administrative boundary between an operator's network twin (Tran et al., 2025; Almasan et al., 2022) and a municipality's federated city twin (International Telecommunication Union, 2023). This is a standards-integration question, not a research-invention question, and it is answerable within a two-year joint catalyst program.

**Fifth, world-action-model datasets from instrumented infrastructure.** Integrated sensing and communication makes the network itself a city-scale sensor (Liu et al., 2022; International Telecommunication Union, 2023 [ITU-R M.2160]), and RAN digital twins already generate site-specific, physics-grounded training environments (NVIDIA, 2024). Instrumented districts that log state, action, and outcome — the closed-loop tuples that autonomous operation produces as exhaust (TM Forum, IG1230, 2022) — constitute a dataset class that does not yet exist in curated, privacy-preserving, cross-city form. Whether such datasets can train action-capable models of urban infrastructure is an open question; we flag it as a projection, not a claim. But the window to specify the logging schema, consent architecture, and sovereignty controls (Open & Agile Smart Cities & Living-in.EU Technical Sub-Group, 2024; Cirillo et al., 2024) is now, before a decade of operational data accumulates in incompatible formats.

TM Forum brings the autonomy levels, the intent lifecycle, and an operator community that has learned to certify its own claims. The Digital Twin Consortium brings composability frameworks, cross-sector twin architecture, and testbeds — Q-POD and Q-SMART among them — where these five work items can be exercised against real constraints rather than slideware. Neither organization can close the gap alone, because the gap is precisely the seam between their domains.

The Gemini Principles observed that the value of infrastructure data lies in federation, curation, and evolution over decades (Bolton et al., 2018). Networks are refreshed on roughly ten-year standardization cadences (3GPP, 2025); cryptographic transitions run on similar horizons (National Security Agency, 2022/2025); the built environment a city twin describes persists far longer than either. This is the echo. A schema chosen in 2026 constrains what questions a city can ask of itself in 2046; a cipher suite chosen in 2026 determines whether 2026's data is still confidential in 2046; an interface left proprietary in 2026 becomes the integration project that consumes 2036's budget. Infrastructure architecture is the longest-lived decision a city makes, and unlike buildings, its consequences are invisible until composed against — at which point they reverberate. The three cities are already here, unevenly distributed. Which one becomes evenly distributed is decided at the interface, and the interface is being written now.
